# Aligning Sourcing to Demand

*Sourcing involves strategic processes such as deciding whether to make or buy goods or services and setting category strategies to allow the vital few procured items to receive more attention than the trivial many. In addition, the procurement process involves selecting and working with suppliers.*
---

## Make-Versus-Buy, Outsourcing, and Offshoring

### Sourcing Road Map

The **Sourcing Road Map** reframes sourcing as a **strategic capability**, not a purchasing task. Historically, sourcing focused on issuing specifications and obtaining the lowest price. This model treats suppliers as interchangeable and transactional.

Modern sourcing instead:
    
* Views suppliers as **strategic partners**
* Involves **early supplier engagement** in product design, materials selection, and capacity planning
* Emphasizes **long‑term value**, not short‑term unit price reductions

Key strategic shifts:

* From “tell suppliers what to do” &rarr; **two‑way collaboration**
* From isolated buying decisions &rarr; **portfolio‑level category strategies**
* From static contracts &rarr; **dynamic relationships enabled by technology**

Technology (SRM platforms, analytics, e‑sourcing tools) removes administrative burden, allowing sourcing teams to:

Focus on risk management
Optimize supply continuity
Support enterprise‑wide goals such as growth, sustainability, and agility

The roadmap also establishes sourcing as a bridge between strategic planning (supply plans) and execution (procurement).

---
### Sourcing Process

The seven‑step sourcing process is iterative, not linear. Each step informs — and is informed by — the others.

#### 1. Develop / Validate Supply Plan
This is the strategic anchor.

* Aligns sourcing with **product lifecycle changes**
* Accounts for new launches, retirements, geographic expansion
* Uses tools like make‑versus‑buy analysis and offshoring assessment

Supply plans are often run as formal projects with charters, timelines, and cross‑functional teams.

#### 2. Research the Supply Market

This step expands understanding beyond incumbent suppliers.

* Assesses **capacity, cost structures, risks, and innovation**
* Includes **total cost of ownership (TCO)** analysis
* Identifies supply constraints or monopolistic risks

Failure here leads to over‑dependence on familiar suppliers.

#### 3. Create / Refine Categories
Spend is grouped meaningfully:

* Raw materials
* Contract manufacturing
* Logistics
* IT services

Each category gets a **distinct strategy** (e.g., lock‑in partnerships, competitive bidding, dual sourcing).

#### 4. Spend & Portfolio Analysis

Spend analysis identifies:

* High‑dollar categories (Pareto logic)
* Vendor concentration risk

Portfolio analysis evaluates:

* Strategic importance vs. supply difficulty
* Categories fall into **leverage, bottleneck, strategic, or commodity**

#### 5. Supplier Segmentation

Not all suppliers deserve the same relationship.

* Strategic suppliers require collaboration, data sharing, and joint improvement
* Transactional suppliers require efficiency and compliance

Segmentation avoids **over‑managing** low‑value suppliers.
#### 6. Conduct Procurement Processes
This is execution:

* RFQs / RFPs
* Negotiation
* Contracting

But decisions are guided by the **earlier strategic groundwork**.

#### 7. Continuous Improvement

Lessons learned feed back into:

* Category strategies
* Supplier relationships
* Risk mitigation

Without this loop, sourcing stagnates.


#### Procurement Process

Procurement executes sourcing strategy, but it carries **both strategic and tactical responsibilities**.

##### Strategic Procurement Roles:

* Build sourcing risk portfolios
* Conduct make‑versus‑buy analyses
* Decide when to outsource or offshore
* Negotiate long‑term agreements
* Align procurement outcomes with corporate KPIs

These roles require senior experience and cross‑functional authority.

##### Tactical Procurement Roles:

Purchase order issuance
Supplier follow‑up
Exception resolution
Performance tracking
Compliance monitoring

The key organizational insight:
    
    Procurement fails when everyone is tactical
    Procurement excels when strategy and execution are separated but aligned

#### Developing Sourcing Competency

This section explains **why sourcing talent is now a competitive advantage**.
Modern sourcing professionals must:

* Understand financial statements and cost models
* Interpret analytics and dashboards
* Navigate global cultural, legal, and tax differences
* Manage suppliers as **business partners**, not vendors

Soft skills matter just as much as technical skills:

* Cultural sensitivity
* Negotiation diplomacy
* Cross‑functional facilitation

As globalization increases complexity, mediocre sourcing talent translates directly into **missed savings, higher risk, and lost innovation**.

### Make-Versus-Buy Analysis

Make‑versus‑buy is one of the **most consequential supply chain decisions**.

It is:

* **Strategic**: shapes capabilities, cost structure, and reputation
* **Tactical**: revisited as market conditions change

The analysis asks:

* Is the activity core to competitive advantage?
* Can suppliers perform better due to specialization?
* What knowledge might be lost?
* What is the true total cost?

The decision is rarely binary — many firms choose hybrids.

#### Core Competency Analysis

The Kuglin framework ensures decisions are **market‑driven**, not internally biased.

Core insights:

* Companies often *think* they have core competencies customers don’t value
* Outsourcing something customers care deeply about is dangerous

Integral components (high coupling, high risk) should usually be retained.
Modular components (interchangeable, standardized) are safer to outsource.

#### Loss of Skills & Knowledge (Hidden Cost)

Outsourcing can hollow out an organization’s future:

* Loss of engineering insight
* Reduced innovation potential
* Supplier dependency

Toyota’s example shows **selective outsourcing**:

* Retain design
* Outsource execution
* Preserve learning while leveraging scale

This balances efficiency with strategic control.

#### Landed Cost & Total Cost of Ownership

Price ≠ cost.
Landed cost includes:

* Transportation
* Tariffs
* Handling

TCO adds:

* Lead time risk
* Inventory carrying costs
* Quality issues
* Compliance and monitoring costs

Low‑price sourcing often fails when **indirect costs overwhelm savings**.

### Contracting & Outsourcing 

Outsourcing replaces internal capacity; contracting provides capabilities not previously held.
Organizations outsource across:

* Manufacturing
* Logistics
* IT
* CRM
* SRM

The trend reflects **specialization economics** — firms focus where they add unique value.

#### Benefits vs. Complexities of Contracting

##### Benefits:

* Scale efficiencies
* Risk sharing
* Capital flexibility
* Faster innovation

##### Complexities

* Governance overhead
* IP theft risk
* Reputation exposure
* Compliance enforcement

The company remains **accountable to the customer**, regardless of outsourcing.

### Offshoring (Strategic, Not Just Cheap Labor)

Offshoring can:

* Reduce costs
* Open markets
* Diversify risk

But it massively increases:

* Cultural complexity
* Legal exposure
* Control requirements

Nearshoring exists to balance **cost savings with responsiveness**.

#### Offshoring Risk Management

Risks include:

* Supply interruptions
* Infrastructure failures
* Political instability
* Ethical and sustainability violations

Mitigation strategies:

* Inventory buffers
* Redundant suppliers
* Pilot programs
* Enhanced monitoring

Outsourcing does not outsource responsibility.


#### Overseas Manufacturing & Assembly Comparison

The final analysis weighs:

* Cost vs. agility
* Risk vs. savings
* Reputation vs. efficiency

Sometimes final assembly remains local to:

* Reduce tariffs
* Improve responsiveness
* Support brand perception


## Sourcing Requirements and Total Costs

* Once a make‑versus‑buy decision is made, the organization must:
    * Clearly define sourcing requirements
    * Determine delivery timing
    * Evaluate total costs, not just price

* Sourcing requirements vary significantly by:
  * Product type
  * Service type
  * Strategic importance


* More detailed cost analysis becomes possible as:
    * Supplier options are identified
    * More information becomes available

Costs can be determined by three factors: cost of current logistics functions, potential savings in time, and improved quality of service.



### Sourcing Road Map

The **Sourcing Road Map** explains how sourcing has evolved from a transactional purchasing activity into a **strategic, collaborative process**. Instead of simply issuing specs and negotiating price, modern sourcing focuses on strategically selecting supply partners and working with them over time.

Key points:

* Driven by **strategic sourcing** and **supplier relationship management (SRM)**
* Suppliers are engaged in:

    * Product design
    * Material selection
    * Capacity and timing discussions


* Enabled by technology that reduces administrative work and increases visibility
* Emphasizes prioritizing **strategically important items** over “routine” purchases

The roadmap frames sourcing as a **bridge between supply planning and procurement execution**.

### Sourcing Process

This section outlines a seven‑step sourcing process, emphasizing that it is iterative, not linear.


**1. Develop or validate the supply plan**

* Align sourcing with product launches, retirements, and business changes
* Uses tools like make‑versus‑buy and offshoring analysis

**2. Research the supply market**

* Analyze supplier availability, timing, capacity, and total cost
* Look beyond current suppliers

**3. Create or refine categories and category strategies**

* Group spend into logical categories
* Develop tailored sourcing approaches per category

**4. Spend and portfolio analysis**

* Spend analysis identifies high‑value categories and suppliers
* Portfolio analysis evaluates strategic importance vs. supply risk

**5. Supplier segmentation**

* Determines the appropriate level of relationship with each supplier
* Not all suppliers require deep collaboration

**6. Conduct procurement processes**

* Execute sourcing decisions through RFQs, negotiations, and contracts

**7. Continuous improvement**

* Uses feedback to improve future sourcing plans and supplier strategies

### Procurement Process

The procurement process supports sourcing by handling **day‑to‑day execution**, but it includes both **strategic and tactical roles**.

**Strategic procurement responsibilities:**

* Identifying and developing strategic suppliers
* Conducting make‑versus‑buy analyses
* Selecting outsourcing and offshoring strategies
* Negotiating long‑term contracts
* Managing sourcing risk and supplier performance

**Tactical procurement responsibilities:**

* Issuing and tracking purchase orders
* Following up on deliveries
* Resolving discrepancies
* Monitoring compliance, quality, and delivery performance

Effective organizations clearly separate strategic and tactical procurement roles.

### Developing Sourcing Competency

This section focuses on the **skills required for modern sourcing professionals**.

Key competencies:

* Understanding overall business strategy, not just purchasing goals
* Strong analytical and financial skills
* Knowledge of global markets, laws, and cultures
* Technology proficiency (SRM, sourcing platforms)

### Core Competency Considerations

A central question in make‑versus‑buy decisions is whether the activity is a **core competency**.
Important ideas:

* **Integral components** (unique and critical) are high-risk to outsource
* **Modular components** (standardized and interchangeable) are lower-risk
* Kuglin’s approach compares:

    * Market need for a capability
    * Organization’s ability to deliver it



Possible outcomes:

* Retain capability internally
* Develop capability internally
* Outsource to a partner with complementary strengths

### Consequences of Lost Skills or Knowledge

Outsourcing can result in the **loss of valuable organizational expertise**.

Risks include:

* Loss of technical knowledge
* Reduced innovation
* Dependency on suppliers

Some organizations mitigate this by:

* Outsourcing execution
* Retaining design or engineering capabilities internally

This allows efficiency gains without fully sacrificing control.

### Landed Cost and Total Cost of Ownership

Once a buy decision is made, cost comparison goes beyond price.

* **Landed cost** includes:

    * Purchase price
    * Transportation
    * Tariffs, duties, and logistics fees


* **Total cost of ownership (TCO)** expands further to include:

    * Quality costs
    * Inventory carrying costs
    * Risk, monitoring, and lifecycle costs

Lower purchase prices often lead to **higher hidden costs**.

"In supply chain management, the total cost of ownership of the supply delivery system is the sum of all the costs associated with every activity of the supply stream. The main insight that TCO offers to the supply chain manager is the understanding that the acquisition cost is often a very small portion of the total cost of ownership."

### Should-Cost Estimate

A **should‑cost estimate** is an **independent estimate of what a product or service ought to cost**, based on its underlying cost drivers—not on what a supplier says it costs or offers as a price. It is used primarily when suppliers **do not disclose detailed cost information**, which is common in global and outsourced supply chains. 1
Rather than asking “What does the supplier charge?”, should‑cost asks:

    “Given how this product or service is made and where it is sourced, what should it reasonably cost?”
