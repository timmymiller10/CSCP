# Demand Management

## Purpose of Demand Analysis
Demand analysis exists because strategic, tactical, and operational plans can fail quickly due to:

* Economic downturns or booms
* Changing customer tastes
* Competitors offering better value at lower prices
* New governments, laws, or regulations

Therefore, organizations must conduct an **environmental scan** and design **flexible supply chain strategies** that can adapt quickly.

**Key idea:**
    
    Demand analysis evaluates the organization in relation to its external environment, not in isolation.


## Demand Analysis by Time Horizon

### Long-Term (Strategic)
* Focuses on the **macro environment**

**Macro environment (ASCM definition):**

    External forces a firm cannot control, including technological, economic, natural, and regulatory forces

* Organizations cannot control the macro environment, but they can adapt products, services, and structure over time
* Primary tool: **SWOT analysis**


### Medium-Term (Tactical)
* Uses **market research**
* Identifies external influences on demand
* Marketing can influence demand at this level through:

    * Pricing
    * Promotions
    * Policy changes
    * Customer incentives

### Short-Term (Operational)

* Focuses on **managing and prioritizing demand**
* Organization can:

    * Allocate limited supply
    * Delay or shift orders
    * Influence customers to modify purchasing behavior during imbalances

## Demand Management in the Supply Chain

Demand must be:

1. **Planned (analyzed and forecasted)**
2. **Communicated**
3. **Influenced**
4. **Prioritized**

* Operations planning and control then:

    * Links **supply with demand**
    * Determines inventory strategies
    * Plans logistics (warehousing, transportation, materials handling)

## Core Supply Chain Processes Related to Demand

### Developing Supply Chain Personnel

Includes:

* Goal setting with managers
* Training and certifications
* Coaching and mentoring
* Ongoing skill assessment

### Developing Supply Chain Infrastructure
For ***Make*** decisions:

* Design manufacturing or service delivery processes
* Develop logistics, information, and funds‑flow systems
* Staff new roles
* Train employees
* Use change management to embed processes in culture

For ***Buy*** decisions:

* Develop supplier relationships
* Develop key customer relationships
* Create, monitor, and control contracts

### Supply and Demand Planning & Scheduling

Includes:

* Building a shared view of demand across functions
* Demand planning and forecasting
* Influencing customers during imbalances
* Master planning (long‑term capacity)
* **Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP)** for consensus plans
* Master scheduling (medium‑term capacity)
* **MRP** (materials)
* **DRP** (distribution)
* **CRP** (capacity)
* Inventory planning
* Warehouse and transportation capacity forecasting
* Reconciling demand, supply, and financial plans

### Identifying Logistical Requirements

Includes:

* Logistics objectives
* Warehousing objectives and capacity
* Materials‑handling requirements
* Transportation objectives and mode selection
* Balancing warehousing vs. transportation trade‑offs


### Developing Logistical Capabilities
* Internal logistics development or
* Outsourcing to logistics service providers (3PLs)
* Includes staffing, training, infrastructure, contracts, and change management

### Executing the Plans
Includes:

* Production activity control
* Capacity control
* Inventory control
* Transportation and delivery monitoring
* Third‑party provider management
* Expediting when needed
* Legal and regulatory compliance

## SWOT Analysis

SWOT = Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats

|Internal|External|
|-|-|
|Stengths|Opportunities|
|Weaknesses|Threats|

* **Strengths & Weaknesses**: internal factors (skills, facilities, reputation)
* **Opportunities & Threats**: external factors (markets, competitors, regulations, technology)

Derived from:

* Internal data
* Customer and supplier feedback
* Environmental scanning

SWOT feeds directly into the **market plan**.

## Market Research & Marketing's Role

### Marketing vs. Sales

* **Marketing**: translates market needs into internal requirements
* **Sales**: translates internal offerings into customer value

Market research can occur:

* At idea/concept stage
* For existing products
* For underperforming products

Tools include:

* Surveys
* Interviews
* Focus groups
* Online feedback
* Purchased market reports

### Types of Market Research

**Market analysis** – size, location, segmentation, growth
**Sales analysis** – sales trends and market share
**Consumer research** – attitudes, preferences, motivations

## Market Plan (Subset of Business Plan)

ASCM definition:

    The output of the market planning process.

Includes:

* Current market position
* Opportunity and issue analysis (SWOT)
* Objectives and strategies
* Action plans and projects
* Budgets
* **Pro forma P&L statement**
* Management controls

**Critical exam link:**

    The market plan strongly influences supply chain design (plant location, postponement, sourcing, logistics).


## Demand Forecasting

* Marketing forecasts are often optimistically biased
* Over‑forecasting → excess capacity and inventory
* Under‑forecasting → lost sales and service failures


Bias is mitigated through:
* Cross‑functional collaboration
* S&OP
* CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment)

## Competition & Benchmarking

Organizations must understand:

* Competitor products and pricing
* Market share by region
* Unmet customer needs
* Competitors’ supply chain maturity

Tools:

* Benchmarking
* SWOT
* Environmental scanning

## Global Perspective on Demand Management
Globalization causes:

* Longer, more complex supply chains
* Higher volatility
* Greater exposure to disruptions

Key points:

* Competition is now supply chain vs. supply chain
* Local market knowledge is essential
* Flexibility and resilience are required

Global disruptions (natural or human‑made) can impact supply chains worldwide.

## Product Portfolio Management

The **product portfolio** is the mix of:

* Product classifications
* Product families
* Individual products
* Services

Must align with:

* Market demand
* Marketing strategy
* Supply chain capabilities

## Product Classifications

**Broad Categories**

* **Durable goods**
* **Non‑durable goods**
* **Services** (intangible, non‑inventoriable)

**Industrial vs. Consumer Goods**

Each has different strategic priorities:
* Cost
* Quality
* Speed
* Dependability
* Flexibility

## Product Portfolio Complexity (SKUs)

* More SKUs:

    * Increase manufacturing complexity
    * Increase purchasing and inventory costs
    * Dilute marketing messages


* Too few SKUs:

    * Lose customers and revenue



**Profit peaks at an optimal SKU level**

Balancing marketing and operations priorities is critical.

## Product Life Cycle (PLC)

Stages:

1. Introduction
2. Growth
3. Maturity
4. Decline

Life‑cycle changes require:

* Adjusted demand plans
* Updated supply chain strategies
* Different pricing and service approaches

## Services Review

Services (warranties, support, training, returns) must be reviewed for value.

* When all competitors offer the same service → **saturation**
* Saturated services become **order qualifiers**
* New services may be needed to stimulate demand

## Brand Strategy & New Product Introduction (NPI)

NPI requires:

* Market analysis
* R&D
* Marketing investment
* Phase‑in / phase‑out planning

Demand forecasting for NPI:

* Relies heavily on **qualitative forecasting**
* High risk of error

Brand focus by life‑cycle stage:

* Growth → availability & quality
* Maturity → price & dependability
* Decline → low‑cost availability

# Influencing Demand

**Influencing demand** refers to the deliberate actions an organization takes to **shape, shift, stimulate, dampen, or redirect customer demand** so that demand better aligns with:

* Available supply
* Capacity constraints
* Strategic objectives
* Profitability goals
* Service-level commitments

Key distinction:

* **Forecasting demand** predicts what customers will do
* **Influencing demand** changes what customers choose to do

Demand is not purely external and uncontrollable—organizations can influence demand at tactical and operational levels.

## Purpose
Organizations influence demand because:

* Supply is constrained (capacity, labor, materials)
* Demand is volatile or uncertain
* Forecast error is unavoidable
* Not all demand is equally profitable
* Serving all demand equally can destroy value

**Core objective:**
Align demand with supply in a way that maximizes total supply chain value, not just sales volume.

## Levels of Demand Influencing

### Strategic Level
* Product and service design
* Market positioning
* Brand strategy
* Pricing architecture
* Channel strategy
### Tactical Level
* Promotions
* Pricing adjustments
* Customer incentives
* Allocation rules
* Market segmentation
### Operational Level
* Order promising
* Lead-time quoting
* Backorder policies
* Demand prioritization
* Substitution and postponement

## Role of Marketing, Sales, and Supply Chain
Effective demand influencing requires **cross‑functional collaboration**, especially through S&OP and IBP (Integrated Business Planning).
### Marketing
* Creates and stimulates demand
* Uses pricing, promotion, product features
* Has a **natural optimistic bias** toward demand
### Sales
* Manages customer relationships
* Negotiates delivery dates and quantities
* May overpromise without supply visibility
### Supply Chain
* Provides **feasibility and constraints**
* Ensures demand is **profitable and executable**
* Influences which demand should be encouraged or discouraged

## Demand Segmentation
Not all demand should be treated equally.
Demand can be segmented by:

Customer profitability
Service requirements
Price sensitivity
Volume predictability
Strategic importance

### Order Qualifiers vs Order Winners
* **Order qualifiers**: Minimum requirements to compete
* **Order winners**: Differentiators that win business

Influencing demand often means:

* Maintaining qualifiers across all customers
* Reserving winners for **high‑value segments**
