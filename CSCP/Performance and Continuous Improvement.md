# Performance and Continuous Improvement

## Operations, Inventory, and Financial Performance

Operations, inventory, and financial performance metrics help ensure that operations goes according to plan, inventory levels stay efficient while being effective at meeting customer service goals, and financial performance is acceptable. Metrics also indicate when plans are not being met so course adjustments can be made. Metrics and key performance indicators (KPIs) are discussed first from a big-picture perspective.

### Metric and KPIs Road Map

Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are the tools used to ensure that supply chain operations stay on plan and financial performance remains acceptable.

#### The Nature of Metrics

The fundamental philosophy of measurement is: **"You get what you measure."** While measuring a goal doesn't guarantee its achievement, failing to measure it almost certainly ensures failure.

* **Objective First**: Decision makers must determine their objectives before selecting criteria for success.
* **Feasibility**: Targets should be challenging but feasible, ideally set in consultation with the people performing the work.
* **Data Integrity**: Metrics rely on accurate, automatically collected data. Without this, executive decisions become guesswork.
* **Actionable Reporting**: Data should be summarized to highlight key areas promptly but allow for a "drill down" to root causes when issues arise.

#### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

While all KPIs are metrics, **not all metrics are KPIs**. A KPI is a specific financial or nonfinancial measure tied directly to an organization's strategy and stakeholders.

**Guidelines for Working with KPIs:**

* **The Rule of 20**: Limit KPIs to a workable number (usually no more than 20) to avoid diluting focus or increasing measurement expense unnecessarily.
* **Balanced Scorecard Approach**: KPIs should cover four general areas:
  * **Business Process Improvement** (e.g., cycle time reduction)
  * **Customer Considerations** (e.g., satisfaction scores)
  * **Financial Impact** (e.g., profitability)
  * **Growth and Learning** (e.g., staff training levels)
* **Strategic Alignment**: A true supply chain KPI must go beyond internal "silos." For example, measuring inventory at only one warehouse is less useful than measuring aggregate inventory across the entire supply chain.

#### Common Supply Chain KPIs by Function

|Function|Examples of KPIs|
|:---|:---|
|**Sourcing**|Price performance, quality, delivery metrics, and innovation.|
|**New Product Introduction**|Internal/external failure rates, scrap, and introduction lead time.|
|**Merchandising**|Market share, volume growth, and supply chain inventory turns.|
|**Replenishment**|Order fill rate, on-time delivery, and forecast accuracy.|

#### Operations and Inventory Performance

Operational measures focus on the efficiency of machines, workers, and processes.

* **Operations KPIs**: Completion of master production schedules, time fence violations (late replanning), and production yield.
* **Inventory KPIs**: 
  * Cost Reduction: Minimizing holding, ordering, and transport costs.
  * Customer Service: Ensuring quality and availability to prevent lost market share.

**The Inventory Tightrope**: Inventory management is a balancing act—keeping enough to meet customer service goals while avoiding the financial burden and risk of obsolescence that comes with carrying too much.

### Operations and Inventory Performance Measures

**Operations and Inventory Performance Measures** act as a counterbalance to one another. While operations metrics often focus on high utilization, inventory metrics ensure that such utilization doesn't lead to wasteful excess.

#### Operations Key Performance Indicators

Operational measurements track the efficiency and throughput of machines, workers, and departments. Key metrics include:

* **Schedule Adherence**: The percentage of the master production schedule completed as planned.
* **Time Fence Violations**: Tracking late and expensive changes to the production plan.
* **Production Yield**: Comparing actual yield against standard or maximum yield to identify scrap, waste, or shrinkage.
* **Inventory Turnover**: Breaking down "turns" by category (raw materials vs. work-in-process) to identify where capital is tied up.

#### The Inventory "Tightrope"

Inventory management is a balancing act between two competing KPIs:

1. **Cost Reduction**: Minimizing the expenses associated with holding, ordering, and transporting goods.
2. **Customer Service**: Maintaining enough stock to ensure high quality, availability, and on-time delivery.

**Risk of Imbalance**: Too little inventory leads to long lead times and lost market share. Too much inventory increases the risk of **distressed goods**, write-offs, and obsolescence, particularly in fast-moving industries.

#### Methods of Tracking Inventory

To prevent **Inventory Shrinkage** (loss due to theft, damage, or evaporation), organizations follow a standard tracking process:

* **Identify the SKU**: Every item is assigned a Stock Keeping Unit (SKU)—a unique code used to track inventory at a specific location.
* **Verify Quantity**: Counting inbound and outbound items against purchase orders.
* **Execute & Record**: Finalizing transactions manually or electronically (using **RFID**) to ensure the system reflects reality.

#### Inventory Audit Metrics

Tracking systems provide two critical data points that must be reconciled:

* **Record on Hand (ROH)**: What the system says is there.
* **Physical on Hand (POH)**: What is actually there.
* **The Gap**: If POH is less than ROH, an inventory record adjustment is required. Failure to do this leads to "phantom inventory," where items are sold online that don't actually exist in the warehouse.

### Fianancial Measures

It is critical for supply chain managers to understand how their operational decisions (Plan, Source, Transform, Order, Fulfill, and Return) translate into high-level financial reporting. This knowledge helps prove that supply chain activities create tangible year-over-year improvements.

#### Financial Statement Elements Impacted by Supply Chain Processes

The following table highlights how each core supply chain process affects the **Balance Sheet** (Statement of Financial Position) and the **Income Statement**:

|Process|Balance Sheet Elements|Income Statement Elements|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Plan**|Raw materials inventory, Accounts payable, Cash, Debt|Cost of goods sold (COGS)|
|**Source**|Property, plant, and equipment (PPE), Accounts payable, Cash, Debt|Depreciation, Taxes|
|**Transform**|Raw materials and WIP inventory, Accounts payable, Accrued expenses, Wages payable, Cash|COGS, Wages, Utilities|
|**Order**|Accounts receivable, Warranty reserves, Commissions payable|Net sales, Marketing and selling expenses|
|**Fulfill**|Finished goods inventory, Accounts payable, Accrued expenses, Wages payable, Cash|COGS, Wages, Utilities (warehousing)|
|**Return**|Accounts payable, Accounts receivable, Inventory/Warranty reserves, Cash|Net sales (Returns/Allowances)|

#### Key Financial Considerations

* **Asset Management**: Inventory is one of the largest assets on the **Balance Sheet**. Reducing inventory levels directly improves **Cash** flow and reduces **Debt** requirements.
* **The Bottom Line**: Operational efficiencies in the "Transform" and "Fulfill" stages directly lower the **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**, which is the most significant lever for increasing gross profit on the Income Statement.
* **Regulatory Compliance**: In the U.S., the **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** requires organizations to have an adequate system of internal measurements. Aligning supply chain metrics with financial reports helps validate these internal controls for auditors and stakeholders.

## Continuous Improvement

Here we define and describe the purpose of continuous improvement along with a foundational quality system called total quality management. After looking at why a supply chain manager needs to learn about these tools, we describe a continuous improvement process. Some broad continuous improvement tools such as root cause analysis and benchmarking are discussed, and benchmarking gets a deeper dive.

### Continuous Improvement and Total Quality Road Map

**Continuous Improvement (CI)** is the act of making incremental, regular upgrades to a process or product in the search for excellence. When applied specifically to processes, it is known as **Continuous Process Improvement (CPI)**.

#### Total Quality Management (TQM)

TQM is a management approach centered on long-term success through **customer satisfaction**. It relies on the participation of all members of an organization to improve processes, goods, and culture.

**Key Components of Quality:**

* **Quality of Conformance**: Defined by the absence of defects.
* **Quality of Design**: Measured by how well the product's features meet customer expectations.

**Foundational Premises of TQM:**

* **Management Commitment**: Leaders must be role models and active participants.
* **No Finger-Pointing**: The focus is on fixing processes, not blaming individuals.
* **Customer Focus**: Every discussion about improvement starts with the internal or external customer.
* **Supplier Partnerships**: Relationships with suppliers should be collaborative rather than adversarial.

#### The Continuous Improvement (CI) Model

The CI model is a circular, four-stage process used to analyze and implement change:

* **Stage 1: Process Analysis**: Identify internal and external supply chain designs where waste or non-value-added activities occur.
* **Stage 2: Process Assessment**: Review KPIs and use benchmarking to set specific performance goals (e.g., reducing lead time by 20%).
* **Stage 3: Project Planning**: Create a realistic schedule, budget, and resource plan. Assign accountability and gather stakeholder commitment.
* **Stage 4: Implementation and Change Management**: Execute the design, gather feedback, and communicate new roles and responsibilities to all entities.

#### Personnel Improvement

Investing in the knowledge, skills, and abilities of the workforce reduces learning curves and increases consistency.

* **Coaching & Mentoring**: Superiors provide feedback (coaching), or experienced managers guide less-experienced peers (mentoring).
* **Cross-Training**: Training employees to perform multiple roles, increasing organizational flexibility.
* **Learning Styles**: Training should account for **Visual** (graphics), **Tactile** (hands-on), and **Auditory** (verbal) learners.

### Continuous Improvement Tools

Continuous Improvement (CI) teams use a variety of qualitative and quantitative techniques to refine processes. While there are many specialized tools, the text highlights four primary methods for identifying and eliminating waste.

#### Core Continuous Improvement Tools

* **Pattern Identification**: This involves pinpointing specific patterns of variability within a process. By understanding when and why a process deviates from the standard, managers can stabilize performance.
* **Defect Measurement**: This identifies the specific number of defects that represent product or service failures. It provides the data necessary to prioritize which issues are most damaging to customer satisfaction.
* **Root Cause Analysis (RCA)**: This is the process of identifying the fundamental reason for a problem rather than just treating its symptoms. The goal is to ensure the problem does not recur by fixing the underlying "root" failure.
* **Benchmarking**: This sets performance goals by referencing an outside standard, such as a competitor's metrics or industry "best practices."

#### Benchmarking Strategies

Benchmarking is one of the most powerful CI tools because it provides a "reality check" for what is achievable. The reading identifies three broad approaches:

|Approach|Description|Example|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Competitive**|Comparing performance directly against a rival in the same industry.|Ford benchmarking its accounts payable against Mazda to reduce personnel.|
|**Best-in-Class**|Looking for the best performer in any industry for a specific process.|A hospital clinic using Toyota’s lean manufacturing methods to reduce patient backlogs.|
|**Process**|Comparing a process against a checklist of world-class qualitative features.|Using the Oliver Wight checklist to evaluate if strategy drives your supply chain execution.|

#### Root Cause Analysis & "Facts are Your Friend"

The reading stresses that effective CI relies on data-driven decision-making.

* **The "As-Is" State**: Before a process can be improved, it must be mapped in its current state using hard data.
* **The "To-Be" State**: This is the proposed, optimized version of the process designed to eliminate the root causes of inferior performance.
* **Low-Hanging Fruit**: Teams are encouraged to target "fast-fix" projects early on. These small wins build the confidence and expertise needed to eventually tackle more complex, multi-entity supply chain disruptions.

## Quality Tools

### Seven Basic Tools of Quality

**Seven Basic Tools of Quality** (also known as the **B7**) are a set of graphical and statistical techniques used to understand, monitor, and improve organizational processes.

#### 1. Process Map (Flowchart)

A process map is a visual diagram of the flow of a production or service process. It uses standardized symbols to show processing steps, branching decisions, and inputs/outputs.

* **Purpose**: Helps teams identify inefficient steps, locate problem areas, and understand the duration and resources required for each task.
* **Tip**: Teams often use brainstorming sessions with sticky notes to map out the "as-is" state of a process.

#### 2. Control Chart
This tool provides a graphic comparison of process performance against predetermined statistical limits.

* **Components**: A center line (mean) and Upper/Lower Control Limits (UCL and LCL) usually set at $\pm 3$ sigmas.
* **Function**: Differentiates between **common cause variation** (inherent in the process) and **special cause variation** (unusual disruptions). If points fall within the limits, the process is "in control."

#### 3. Pareto Chart

Based on **Pareto’s Law** (the 80-20 rule), this bar graph displays results in descending order of frequency or impact.

* **Logic**: It separates the **"vital few"** causes from the **"trivial many."** For example, 80% of defects might come from only 20% of the possible causes.
* **Application**: Use this to decide where to focus limited resources for the biggest gains.

#### 4. Cause-and-Effect Diagram (Fishbone/Ishikawa)

This diagram links a specific problem (the "effect") to its potential causes, organized into categories like Environment, People, Materials, Measurement, Methods, and Equipment.

* **Root Cause Analysis**: The goal is to move past superficial symptoms to find the underlying failure.
* **The Five Whys**: A Japanese method used alongside this diagram, suggesting that asking "Why?" five times will eventually lead to the real cause.

#### 5. Histogram

A bar graph that shows the **frequency distribution** of measurement data (like weight or temperature) over a continuous range.

* **Shape Analysis**: A bell curve indicates a normal distribution. "Twin peaks" might indicate data coming from two different sources (like two different shifts), while skewed data shows a bias toward one end of the range.

#### 6. Check Sheet

A simple tally sheet used to record the frequency of event occurrences, such as specific defects, in real-time.

* **Usage**: It is often the starting point for data collection before moving into more complex analysis like Pareto or Histograms. Data must be representative and collected from a single entity (like one specific machine) to be accurate.

#### 7. Scatter Chart (Scatterplot)

A tool used to show the relationship between two variables to see if they are interdependent.

* **Correlation**: If the points trend upward to the right, there is a **positive correlation** (e.g., more training hours leading to higher competency).
* **Predictive Value**: These are often used in **associative forecasting** to predict how a dependent variable will behave based on changes to an independent one.

### Seven New Tools

#### 1. Affinity Diagram

This tool is used to organize a large number of brainstormed ideas into natural groups based on their relationships.

* **Process**: Participants provide anonymous ideas on cards, which are then clustered by the group into categories with descriptive titles.
* **Purpose**: Provides a "big picture" view of a complex issue (e.g., identifying broad causes for a product recall).

#### 2. Tree Diagram

A tree diagram breaks down a high-level goal into increasingly finer levels of detail to create an actionable plan.

* **Structure**: The main goal is on the left, with branches "blooming" to the right to clarify tasks, costs, and requirements.
* **Example**: Mapping the development of a new product by branching into price, durability, and design categories.

#### 3. Matrix Diagram

This tool shows the relationship between two or more groups of information, including the strength of those interactions.

* **L-Shaped Matrix**: The most common form, used to compare two groups (e.g., comparing specific product specifications against the requirements of different customers).

#### 4. Process Decision Program Chart (PDPC)

The PDPC is used to visually capture what might go wrong in a plan.

* **Proactive Planning**: It helps identify measures to avoid undesirable consequences or to design "Plan B" contingencies for foreseeable problems.

#### 5. Relationship Diagram (Interrelationship Digraph)

This diagram illustrates cause-and-effect relationships among various aspects of a complicated issue.

* **Analysis**: You draw arrows from an idea to others it influences. The "major cause" is the one with the most outgoing arrows, while the "most interrelated" cause has the most total arrows (in and out).

#### 6. Matrix Data Analysis Chart

While similar to a matrix diagram, this tool uses numerical data to show the relationship between variables.

* **Comparison**: It is often used to differentiate product lines or marketing messages by plotting them against two specific features (e.g., opacity vs. insulating properties).

#### 7. Activity Network Diagram

Also known as a **Critical Path Method (CPM)** chart, this tool conveys dependencies and simultaneous activities.

* **Project Management**: It helps teams identify the required order of tasks and calculate the "critical path"—the sequence of activities that determines the minimum project duration.

#### Choosing the Right Tool

The reading suggests categorizing these 14 tools (Basic and New) to match your objective:

* **Nonquantitative**: Process maps, fishbone diagrams, affinity diagrams, tree diagrams, and relationship diagrams.
* **Quantitative**: Scatter charts, Pareto charts, histograms, and control charts.
* **Combination**: Matrix diagrams (when using measured data points).

## Continuous Improvement Methods

A number of continuous improvement philosophies such as lean, Just-in-Time (JIT), six sigma, and the theory of constraints are discussed here to show how continuous improvement can be embraced systematically. First there is an introduction to these methods.

### Continuous Improvement Methods Road Map

#### Overview of Key Philosophies

These methods are most effective when combined under a single continuous improvement "umbrella":

* **Lean**: Focuses on building quality directly into products and processes. It emphasizes waste elimination and relies on empowered, multiskilled teams.
* **Just-in-Time (JIT)**: Closely related to Lean, JIT focuses on reducing inventories to zero. The goal is for products to appear exactly when needed—never before and never after.
* **Six Sigma**: Focuses on the customer experience by reducing process variation. The specific goal is to reach a level of statistical insignificance in defects (near-perfect quality).
* **Theory of Constraints (TOC)**: A systematic method for increasing throughput by identifying and fixing the "slowest link" (the constraint) in a system.

#### How They Work Together

The reading suggests these approaches are complementary rather than competing:

1. **TOC** gets the system running at the necessary speed to meet demand.
2. **Lean tactics** reduce waste and optimize the timing of deliveries. 
3. **Six Sigma** controls smooth out any remaining variability to ensure consistency.

#### The Concept of Waste

A central theme in both Lean and JIT is the relentless pursuit of waste elimination. Waste is defined in two ways:

1. Any activity that **does not add value** in the eyes of the consumer.
2. A by-product of a task that requires special management control (like hazardous waste).

##### The 8 Deadly Wastes

Toyota originally identified seven wastes, with an eighth (unused people skills) added later.

|Type|Description|
|:---|:---|
|**Process**|Inefficient steps or unneeded work to produce an output.|
|**Movement**|Moving products unnecessarily (transportation).|
|**Methods**|Wasted time or "motion" by operators.|
|**Product Defects**|Services or products that fail to meet specifications.|
|**Waiting Time**|Delays or queuing.|
|**Overproduction**|The most serious waste—making more than is required.|
|**Excess Inventory**|Holding stock that is not needed for current orders.|
|**Unused People Skills**|Failing to use the knowledge or capabilities of the workforce.|


### Lean

**Lean** is a production philosophy focused on minimizing the use of all resources—including time—by relentlessly identifying and eliminating non-value-adding activities.

Originally integrated by Henry Ford as "flow production" and later refined by Toyota into the **Toyota Production System (TPS)**, Lean emphasizes flexibility, multiskilled teams, and high-quality results.

#### Core Lean Objectives

Lean supply chains aim for specific operational benchmarks:

* **Increase Velocity**: Maximizing the speed of transactions to improve asset turnover and customer response times.
* **Reduce Working Capital**: Freeing up cash by maintaining lower inventory levels.
* **Increase Inventory Turns**: Using methods like assemble-to-order to cycle inventory more frequently.
* **Develop the Workforce**: Empowering employees with more responsibility and cross-training to handle diverse tasks.

#### The Five Lean Principles

Womack and Jones defined five pillars that guide lean thinking:

1. **Create Value**: Value can only be defined by the customer; it is what they are willing to pay for.
2. **Identify the Value Stream**: Map every step (value-added and non-value-added) from raw material to finished product.
3. **Create Value Flow**: Strive for a continuous, smooth flow of products to make problems visible.
4. **Pull Based on Demand**: Only produce what the downstream customer asks for (Make-to-Order).
5. **Strive for Perfection**: Continuously remove layers of waste as they are uncovered.

#### The House of Toyota

This framework explains the scope of Lean using the metaphor of a stable house:

* **The Roof (Goals)**: Best quality, lowest cost, and shortest lead time.
* **The Pillars**: Supported by **Just-in-Time (JIT)** and **Jidoka** (automation with a human touch).
* **The Foundation**: Built on **Operational Stability**, which includes Heijunka (load leveling) and Standardized Work.
* **The Center**: A culture of **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)** and **Respect for People**.

#### Key Lean Tools and Techniques

* **Value Stream Mapping**: A simple paper-and-pencil tool to visualize the flow of materials and information, specifically separating value-added time from non-value-added time.
* **The Five Ss**: A workplace organization method: Sort, Simplify, Scrub, Standardize, and Sustain.
* **Total Productive Maintenance (TPM)**: Engaging all employees to maximize equipment effectiveness and prevent accidents or breakdowns.
* **Setup Time Reduction**: A five-step process to reduce the time a machine is idle during changeovers, which directly reduces lead times and allows for smaller lot sizes.

#### Types of Waste: Muda, Mura, and Muri

Taiichi Ohno identified three broad categories of waste that management must address:

* **Muda**: Traditional waste (the 8 deadly wastes like overproduction and waiting).
* **Mura**: Unevenness or inconsistency in demand or processes.
* **Muri**: Overburdening employees or equipment, often caused by trying to "catch up" to forecasted numbers.

### Just-in-Time (JIT)

**Just-in-Time (JIT)** is a manufacturing philosophy that focuses on the planned elimination of all waste and the continuous improvement of productivity. While it is a pillar of Lean, it can also function as a standalone strategy.

The primary goal of JIT is to ensure the **right items** arrive at the **right place** at the **right time** in the right amounts.

#### The Three Basics of JIT

* **Waste Reduction**: JIT targets non-value-added activities such as storage, inspections, and defects for step-by-step elimination.
* **Variability Reduction**: Focuses on removing internal or external inconsistencies, such as inaccurate drawings or unreliable equipment.
* **Pull vs. Push**: In traditional "push" systems, materials move based on preset schedules and forecasts. JIT "pulls" items through the system only when demanded by the next workstation, which quickly highlights quality issues.

#### Elements of JIT Continuous Improvement

To function correctly, JIT requires specific changes across several supply chain areas:

* **Suppliers**: JIT requires long-term relationships with a few high-quality suppliers willing to deliver frequent, small shipments with zero defects.
* **Layout**: Facilities are often arranged in work cells (families of products) to minimize travel distance. Equipment is often kept movable rather than bolted to the floor.
* **Inventory Reduction**: JIT views inventory as a "mask" that hides problems. By reducing safety stock to a minimum, any variability or defect becomes immediately visible, forcing a process improvement.
* **Scheduling**: JIT uses two primary scheduling types:
  * **Level Schedules**: Producing small batches of changing items so daily production exactly matches daily demand.
  * **Kanban**: A Japanese signaling system (using cards, lights, or empty bins) to indicate that a downstream station is ready for more material.

#### Impact on Production Costs

JIT strategies directly influence financial and operational performance through:

* **Lead Time Reduction**: Reducing setup times lowers direct costs and allows for smaller lot sizes.
* **Quality Improvement**: Smaller lot sizes mean defects are identified before they are replicated across a large batch.
* **Backflushing**: An automated accounting method that triggers the deduction of inventory based on the actual number of finished units produced and their bill of materials, reducing administrative ordering costs.

#### Continuous Job Improvement

In a JIT environment, the responsibility for process improvement is shifted to the **workers closest to the process**. This requires:

* **Cross-training**: Expanding employee skills so they can handle diverse tasks.
* **Job Enrichment**: Giving employees more supervisory responsibility and ownership over their workspac####

### Six Sigma

**Six Sigma** is a methodology that uses a set of management tools and techniques to improve business processes by reducing process variation and decreasing the probability of defects.

The ultimate goal is to achieve "near-perfect" quality, defined as no more than **3.4 defects per million opportunities**.

#### Core Elements of Six Sigma

Attaining this level of quality requires a balanced focus on three key areas:

* **The Customer**: Quality is defined by the customer’s mindset. Any failure to meet expectations (reliability, price, on-time delivery) is considered a defect that might drive them to a competitor.
* **The Process**: Organizations must take an "outside-in" view, mapping processes to ensure consistent performance. The goal is to reduce variability so that performance stays as close to the target as possible.
* **The Employee**: Six Sigma requires full participation from the top down. Specialized training leads to certification "belts" (Green, Black, and Master Black Belt), which designate the level of expertise in leading process improvement projects.

#### The DMAIC Process

For existing processes, Six Sigma uses a five-phase improvement cycle known as **DMAIC**:

1. **Define**: Determine the nature of the problem.
2. **Measure**: Record data and facts about underlying causes and current performance.
3. **Analyze**: Study the information to determine the root causes.
4. **Improve**: Effect solutions to solve the problem.
5. **Control**: Monitor the process until the solutions are ingrained in the culture.

**Note**: For creating new processes, a similar model called DMADV (Define, Measure, Analyze, Design, Verify) is used.

#### Six Sigma vs. Traditional Quality

Traditional quality goals often set a limit at **three sigmas**, which corresponds to a 99.74% success rate. While this sounds high, it actually allows for **66,807 defects per million**. Six Sigma raises the bar significantly to 99.9997% accuracy, which is necessary for high-stakes industries like electronics or pharmaceuticals.

#### Lean Six Sigma

The reading highlights the compatibility of these methods through a "Lean Six Sigma" approach. In this hybrid model:

* **Lean** identifies and removes waste and non-value-added steps.
* **Six Sigma** identifies and reduces the remaining variability in the streamlined process.

### Theory of Constraints

The **Theory of Constraints (TOC)**, developed by Eliyahu M. Goldratt, is based on the premise that every system has at least one specific element that limits its maximum output.

#### The Core Philosophy

A constraint is any factor—people, equipment, a process, or even a mindset—that prevents a system from achieving a higher level of performance. TOC suggests that because the constraint dictates the maximum output of the entire chain, it should be the primary focus of all improvement efforts.

* **Throughput**: Speeding up processes before or after a constraint provides no benefit; it only creates excess inventory (if before) or idle time (if after).
* **The Goal**: The system must ensure the constraint is always operating at maximum speed to meet customer demand.

#### The Five-Step TOC Process

TOC is an inherently continuous strategy because once one constraint is broken, a new one will inevitably appear elsewhere in the system.

1. **Identify the Constraint**: Find the "bottleneck" where work is accumulating in a queue.
2. **Exploit the Constraint**: Ensure the constraint is operating at its maximum possible output without making major investment (eg., ensuring it doesn't sit idle during lunch breaks).
3. **Subordinate Everything Else**: Adjust all non-constraints to the pace of the bottleneck. This often involves maintaining **buffers** of material immediately in front of the constraint so it never runs out of work.
4. **Elevate the Constraint**: If output is still insufficient, invest in major improvements like new equipment, more staff, or a complete process redesign.
5. **Repeat the Cycle**: Go back to Step 1 to identify the next limiting factor.

#### Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR)

This is the method used to control the flow of work through a TOC-managed system:

* **The Drum**: The "beat" or pace of the system. It is set by the maximum rate of the constraint (not to exceed the rate of customer demand, or takt time).
* **The Buffer**: A protection of time or materials placed in front of the constraint to ensure that if a minor disruption occurs upstream, the "drum" keeps beating.
* **The Rope**: The communication mechanism (scheduler) that releases work into the system. It "pulls" orders based on the drum's beat, ensuring that work is only started when the constraint can handle it.

#### Lean vs. TOC

While Lean seeks to eliminate all inventory, TOC purposely maintains buffers at the constraints. However, the reading notes that these methods are highly complementary: TOC gets the system running at the correct speed, while Lean tactics can then be used to reduce waste and optimize delivery timing within that stabilized flow.
