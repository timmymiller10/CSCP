# Distribution Services and Delivery Patterns

## Warehouse Activities

### Inbound Processes
* **Receiving**: This is the initial physical receipt of material. It involves inspecting the shipment against the purchase order for quantity and damage, identifying the goods, and preparing receiving reports.
* **Prepackaging**: Products received in bulk are repackaged into smaller quantities or combined with other items to create kits or assortments.
* **Put-away**: The process of moving material from the receiving dock to a staging area and then to a specific storage location. This also includes recording the exact identification and location of the goods in the system.
### Inventory Management
* **Storing**: This involves placing items under warehouse control at a storage point upstream from where they will be used or picked.
* **Move**: The physical transportation of inventory between different locations within the facility, typically directed by the inventory management system.
### Outbound Processes
* **Order Picking (Order Selection)**: The retrieval of a specific quantity of products from storage to fill shipping orders. Common methods mentioned include batch picking, discrete order picking, and zone picking.
* **Shipping**: The final outbound function which includes:
  * **Packaging**: Using materials to protect items during storage and transport.
  * **Packing and Marking**: Unitizing items into containers, weighing them, and labeling them with necessary shipment data for identification and tracking.

## Warehouse Functions and Value-Added Services
### Consolidation and Break-Bulk
* **Consolidation**: The warehouse receives materials from multiple plants and combines them into a single, full containerload (CL) or truckload (TL) for a specific customer. This reduces shipping costs and dock congestion.
* **Break-Bulk**: The opposite of consolidation; a large, bulk shipment is divided into smaller quantities for easier use or for distribution to multiple customers.

### Cross-Docking
This is a high-efficiency technique where products are moved directly from the receiving dock to the shipping dock without ever being stored in inventory. It requires precise coordination and information systems but significantly reduces handling costs and lead times.

### Postponement
In this model, goods enter the warehouse as components rather than finished products. Final assembly is "postponed" until a specific customer order is received.

* **Example**: Storing generic printers and only adding the specific power cord, manual, and language-appropriate labels once the destination country is known.

### Strategic Inventory Stocking
* **Anticipation (Stockpiling)**: Storing seasonal inventory (like lawn furniture or agricultural products) to handle surges in demand without needing to increase year-round production capacity.
* **Spot-Stocking**: Placing inventory in strategic markets just before a period of heavy demand to ensure products are physically close to customers when they are needed most.

### Assortment and Mixing
* **Assortment**: Storing a wide variety of goods close to the customer to ensure short lead times and reduce the number of suppliers the customer has to deal with.
* **Mixing**: A variation of break-bulk where a warehouse receives full shipments of different products from various manufacturers and mixes them into a customized "product mix" for specific customers.

## Network Configurations

### Supply Channel Factors
These factors focus on how an organization acquires materials or finished goods:

* **Supply Channel Complexity**: Refers to the level of interconnectedness and interdependencies across the network. It requires deciding how many levels ("depth") the channel should have and the level of planning control needed.
* **Supply Integrative Intensity**: Determines the level of integration with suppliers.
* **Supplier Intensity**: Focuses on the number and type of suppliers used.



### Distribution Channel Factors
These factors determine how a producer reaches the market and manages downstream activities:

* **Market Penetration Intensity**: How deeply the producer enters the delivery network to increase market share.

* **Distribution Integrative Intensity**: The level of "Forward Integration" (control over activities reaching the end customer) or "Horizontal Integration" (purchasing intermediaries).

* **Distribution Intensity**: Deciding how many intermediaries to use. The reading identifies four specific strategies:

| Strategy | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Single Source** | Producer maintains full control over delivery, price, and service. |
| **Intensive** | Broadest possible network to reach the maximum number of customers. |
| **Exclusive** | Limits intermediaries by offering exclusive rights or banning competing products. |
| **Selective** | Uses multiple intermediaries but maintains strong control without full responsibility. |

### Primary Distribution Networks
The reading highlights three main comprehensive systems:

* **Manufacturer to DC to Retailer**: The traditional model. It is inventory-intensive but provides strong product availability and high service levels for mass-produced goods.
* **Independent Distributor (Omni-Channel)**: An independent "channel master" buys from multiple manufacturers and aggregates goods for a "one-stop shop" for retailers or direct customers.
* **Independent Aggregator (E-Business)**: Heavily dependent on direct marketing and branded websites (e.g., Amazon or Alibaba). It often uses parcel services for direct shipment but may also leverage local distributors for slow-moving items.
