## Emerging Technology Trends

### Emerging Technologies

#### Computing & Analytics

* **Cloud Computing**: A distributed set of servers and databases accessed via the internet. It offers flexibility through **Software as a Service (SaaS)**, allowing companies to lease complex systems like TMS or WMS rather than buying them, ensuring seamless upgrades and lower implementation costs.
* **Artificial Intelligence (AI) & Machine Learning**: Software capable of self-improvement and reasoning. These tools excel at **pattern detection** (spotting fraud or bottlenecks) and predictive analytics, such as forecasting equipment maintenance needs.
* **Quantum Computing**: Uses quantum physics to map millions of permutations simultaneously. It is radically faster than supercomputers for optimization problems, like finding the most fuel-efficient delivery route among trillions of options in seconds.

#### Visibility & Remote Sensing

* **Sensors & Telematics**: Devices that monitor conditions on a dynamic basis and provide automated data capture. This data often feeds into **Control Towers**—centralized hubs that provide a complete, integrated view of the end-to-end supply chain.
* **Drones**: Autonomous or remote-controlled aircraft used for yard tracking, inventory counts, or inspecting difficult-to-access areas like pipelines.

#### Additive & Automated Manufacturing

* **3D Printing (Additive Manufacturing)**: Constructing objects layer-by-layer from digital models. This enables on-demand manufacturing at the point of need, which is ideal for replacement parts and rapid prototyping.
* **Autonomous & Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVS)**: Material handling networks (like those used in Amazon warehouses) that route freight automatically. They reduce labor costs, improve safety, and use less energy than manual movement.
* **Robotic Process Automation (RPA)**: Using software "bots" to handle repetitive, predefined business tasks, such as automated customer service emails or chat functions.

#### Wearables & Augmented Reality

* **Wearable Technology**: Hands-free, voice, or gesture-activated devices worn by workers. These track activity levels and exact locations within a warehouse.
* **Augmented Reality (AR)**: Overlaying holographic imagery or instructions onto the physical environment. For example, smart glasses can highlight exactly which shelf a worker needs to pick from and in what quantity.

### Intelligent supply chains and enablers

#### The Internet of Things (IoT)

The Internet of Things is an environment where objects (equipment, containers, drones) are provided with unique identifiers and the ability to transfer data over a network without human interaction.

* **Applications**: Real-time track-and-trace of shipping containers via GPS, automatic retail price updates on electronic shelving, and maintenance status reporting for warehouse equipment.
* **Benefits**: Direct integration between the physical world and computer systems, allowing for remote sensing and control.
* **Risks**: A lack of universal security standards creates the risk of devices being hacked for data theft or used in denial-of-service attacks.

#### Blockchain

Blockchain is a distributed ledger system where records (blocks) are linked using cryptography, making them nearly impossible to alter retroactively.

* **Supply Chain Benefits:**

  * **Chain of Custody**: Provides irrefutable evidence of the transfer of goods between parties.
  * **Data Integrity**: Automatically captures and distributes reliable data from IoT devices (e.g., temperature logs in a cold chain).
  * **Counterfeit Prevention**: Validates serial numbers to prevent fake goods from entering the network.

* **Key Platforms**: Notable industry solutions include **TradeLens** (IBM/Maersk) and the **Global Shipping Business Network (GSBN)**.

#### Intelligent Asset Management (IAM) & Digital Twins

IAM uses technology to monitor and control industrial assets throughout their lifecycle.

* **Digital Twin**: An exact virtual replica of a real-world process or product. It allows engineers and managers to simulate, test, and monitor an asset's performance in a digital environment before making physical changes.
* **Collaboration**: Performance data can be shared with the original designers for continuous improvement or with maintenance teams for predictive repairs.

#### Case Study: Shipbuilder’s Technology Road Map

The reading provides a road map for a shipbuilder using these three enablers in unison:

* **Year 1**: Tagging small assets with QR codes and building a "Minimum Viable Product" (MVP) for the blockchain system.
* **Year 2**: Upgrading heavy equipment with RFID and launching predictive maintenance processes.
* **Year 3**: Using advanced analytics to adjust equipment levels based on internal demand patterns.

## Technology Assessment and Implementation

### Technology Audits and Implementation Reviews

#### Technology Audits

A technology audit is a formal analysis performed before making IT investments to mitigate risks and allocate spending effectively.

* **Purpose**: To evaluate current networking capabilities and determine what is needed for the next stage of supply chain development.
* **Compliance**: Audits are critical for complying with regulations like the **U.S. Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** or Canada’s Bill 198 (Csox), which require adequate internal financial reporting and IT controls.
* **Scope**: They test for system availability, security, confidentiality, and integrity. For instance, an audit ensures proper "separation of duties," such as confirming that a person who approves purchase orders cannot also receive goods.
* **Target Audience**: The results are intended for **upper management** or boards of directors, rather than a single department, to ensure cross-enterprise alignment.

#### Post-Implementation Reviews

Once a technology project is completed, a post-implementation review is conducted to determine if the organization received the expected **Return on Investment (ROI)**.

* **Focus on Measurement**: Reviews should focus on items that can be measured and managed.
* **Balanced Scorecard**: Success should be measured using a balanced approach, including financial success, customer satisfaction, and quality.
* **Accountability**: Including the review in the initial project plan ensures managers feel a sense of accountability throughout the process.
* **Lessons Learned**: These reviews capture insights that help improve the success of future technology projects.

#### Common Audit Findings

Audits often reveal that the implementation did not go perfectly, which should be used to create positive organizational change. Typical findings include:

* False promises from software vendors regarding capabilities.
* Failure to achieve the promised level of system integration.
* Underutilization of software features due to inadequate training or resistance to change.

#### Cost of Inaction

External auditors often provide a **benefit-cost study** comparing the cost of continuing with existing "legacy" technology versus the cost of replacement. If prior investments had a negative ROI, auditors may suggest "add-on" tools to help current technology produce a positive return.

### Mitigating Typical IT Risk

#### Make Incremental Improvements

Management is often tempted to solve all organizational problems with one "big bang" project. However, these are difficult to manage and prone to failure.

* **The Strategy**: Take small, incremental steps. This allows the organization to add innovation between stages.
* **Milestones**: Large projects should have "go/no-go" milestones to assess progress before committing further funds.

#### Clearly Define Business Requirements

Because there are thousands of vendors and evaluations can take weeks, business goals must be defined from the start.

* **The Strategy**: Ensure that evaluators use management’s principal criteria as the primary filter for any technology solution.

#### Perform Due Diligence on Proposals

Never rely solely on a vendor's marketing presentation or generic demonstrations.

* **The Strategy**: Interview previous purchasers and use third-party evaluators.
* **Proof of Concept**: Have the vendor prove the software works using your own actual data (e.g., providing sample shipping data to test a transportation scheduler) rather than a canned demo.

#### Control Scope Creep

Scope creep is the uncontrolled expansion of project scope without additional time or funding.

* **The Strategy**: Fully define all activities in written documents.
* **Change Procedures**: Establish a formal, written process for defining and estimating the costs of any additional work requested during the project.

#### Control Excessive Customization

A major dilemma is whether to change business processes to fit the software or customize the software to fit current practices.

* **The 80/20 Rule**: Look for software that meets at least 80% of your needs "out of the box." The remaining 20% can be customized.
* **Total Cost of Ownership**: Choosing organizational change over deep customization leads to easier upgrades, lower training costs, and a lower total cost of ownership.
* **Risk Levels**: Using "blank" data fields provided by the vendor is low risk; altering the raw source code is extremely high risk.

### Technology and Strategic Alignment

#### Technology and Equipment Requirements

Requirements move from high-level strategic goals to specific operational tasks.

* **Strategic Requirements**: Technology must support the company’s competitive priority. For a **low-cost provider**, technology must lower costs by more than the investment. For a **differentiation** strategy, it must support unique features or speed.
* **Tactical Requirements**: In a **mass production** environment, requirements focus on high-volume, specialized equipment. In a **Lean** environment, the focus shifts to flexible, smaller machines that allow for fast changeovers and cross-training of workers.
* **Operational Requirements**: These are detailed specifications, such as "sensors must be durable for harsh environments." These should be written as **SMART goals** (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Time-bound).

#### Technology Gap Analysis

A **gap analysis** assesses the difference between actual current performance and the ideal future state.

* **The Process**: Organizations use tools like **benchmarking** or **voice of the customer** to find where they lag behind competitors. **Process mapping** or **value stream mapping** can then identify exactly which steps in a workflow are causing inefficiencies.
* **Leveraging Existing Tech**: Sometimes a gap can be closed without buying new software, simply by training staff or adding "add-on" tools to current systems.

#### Identifying Limitations Before Implementation

The reading warns that a gap analysis often reveals underlying issues that must be fixed before new technology can succeed:

* **Information Architecture**: Legacy systems that are incompatible or poorly scalable.
* **Human Resources**: Skill gaps in technical expertise or leadership, and policy gaps that restrict job expansion.
* **External Partners**: A lack of willingness from suppliers or customers to share information or investment costs.

**Note**: The cost and time required to fix these organizational or personnel limitations must be included in the overall **technology feasibility analysis**.

### Technology Selection Criteria, Selection, and Cutover

#### Technology Selection Criteria

Organizations evaluate potential technology based on four primary pillars:

* **Competitive Advantage**: Technology provides an "order winner" edge if it is:
  * **Scarce**: Proprietary or first-of-its-kind.
  * **Difficult to Move/Copy/Substitute**: Hard for competitors to replicate quickly.
* **Risk Assessment**: New technology is vulnerable to project mismanagement, misrepresentation by sellers, or shifts in market needs that could render the investment obsolete.
* **Feasibility**: This includes **Financial Feasibility** (using a benefit-cost analysis where a ratio over 1.0 is desired) and **Organizational Feasibility** (considering the **learning curve** and employee acceptance).
* **Validation against Requirements**: Ensuring the solution actually supports strategic goals like Quality, Speed, Dependability, Flexibility, and Cost.

**Key Insight**: Feasibility discussions should always involve the employees whose work will be affected. Their "up-close" experience helps identify issues managers might miss and encourages "ownership" of the new system.

#### Selection of Technology

The selection process typically follows a funnel approach to narrow down vendors:

1. **Request for Information (RFI)**: Used to see the range of available market solutions.
2. **Request for Proposal (RFP) / Invitation to Tender (ITT)**: Sent once requirements are finalized to get formal bids.
3. **Proof of Concept**: It is better to have a vendor prove the technology works using your **actual shipping or order data** rather than a generic, "canned" demonstration.

#### Technology Cutover Plan

The cutover plan details how the old system is retired and the new one is activated. There are three common methods, each with different trade-offs:

|Method|Description|Pros/Cons|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Go-Live Date**|A tight schedule where the old system is turned off and the new one is turned on.|**Efficient** but high risk of downtime if a critical task fails.|
|**Parallel Maintenance**|Maintaining the old system while the new one is in use for a set period.|**Low risk** but expensive due to duplicated labor and data entry.|
|**Rolling Cutover**|Implementation occurs in phases (e.g., one site at a time).|**Reduced risk** and allows for a smaller implementation team.|

#### Maintenance Policies

To ensure technology remains useful over its life cycle, organizations must set clear policies regarding:

* **Testing Protocols**: Regular sampling for quality.
* **Access Restrictions**: Defining who can use the technology and for what parameters.
* **Predictive Analytics**: Using data to schedule maintenance before a breakdown occurs, ideally during changeovers or off-hours to minimize disruption.

### Implementing Technologies Using Project Management

#### Project Management Fundamentals

Unlike regular operations, projects are temporary endeavors with a definite beginning and end. Successful implementations require clear roles:

* **Project Charter**: A formal document signed by executives that appoints a project manager, approves funds, and sets **SMART** success criteria.
* **Project Sponsor**: An executive who maintains enthusiasm and champions the project's importance.
* **RACI Matrix**: A tool used to clarify stakeholder roles:
  * **R**esponsible (does the work)
  * **A**ccountable (owns the success/failure; only one person per task)
  * **C**onsulted (provides subject matter expertise)
  * **I**nformed (kept up to date on progress)

#### The Tuckman Ladder of Team Development

Project teams evolve through five distinct stages of functionality:

1. **Forming**: Low understanding; members are just learning their roles.
2. **Storming**: High conflict and competition as the team questions objectives.
3. **Norming**: Increased trust and motivation; collaboration begins.
4. **Performing**: Peak productivity and efficiency.
5. **Adjourning**: Lessons learned are captured, and the team is disbanded.

#### Traditional (Waterfall) Project Management

This "predictive" approach is used when requirements can be frozen early. It follows a sequential path: **Initiating** $\rightarrow$ **Planning** $\rightarrow$ **Executing** $\rightarrow$ **Closing**.

* **The Planning Phase**: This is the most critical phase. While the highest costs occur during "Executing," the greatest opportunity to affect and reduce total costs is during "Planning."
* **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**: A hierarchical tool that organizes 100% of the project work. If a task isn't in the WBS, it is considered "out of scope."
* **Critical Path Method (CPM)**: Identifies the sequence of tasks that determines the shortest possible project duration. Any delay in a critical path task delays the entire project.

#### Agile Project Management

Agile is used when requirements are expected to change or are unknown at the start.

* **Iterative Cycles (Sprints)**: Work is completed in short increments (typically 2–4 weeks), resulting in a "minimum viable product" that can be tested immediately.
* **Scrum Roles**: Includes the **Product Owner** (voice of the customer), the **Scrum Master** (facilitator), and the **Team** (self-organizing developers).
* **Backlog**: A prioritized list of "User Stories" (requirements) that the team pulls from for each sprint.

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

* **Scope Creep**: The uncontrolled expansion of project scope without additional time or funding.
* **Micromanagement**: Failing to delegate responsibility effectively.
* **Static Documentation**: Allowing plans to become out of date rather than treating them as "live" documents.

### Supply Chain Digital Transformation Using Change Management

#### The Necessity of Change Management

Because digital transformation often faces resistance from organizational culture or stakeholders comfortable with the status quo, **Change Management** is a critical enabler. Resistance is especially high if previous initiatives were treated as temporary projects rather than permanent shifts in routine.

**Prerequisites for Change (The Three Habits):**

* **Desire**: The organization must want to change.
* **Knowledge**: Understanding why the change is beneficial and what the end result looks like.
* **Skill**: Acquiring the right tools and techniques to achieve the goals.

#### Kotter’s Eight Accelerators

The reading highlights John P. Kotter’s model for turning a "Big Opportunity" into rapid action through a network organizational structure:

1. **Create Urgency**: Build buy-in and enthusiasm around a big opportunity.
2. **Form a Guiding Coalition**: Assemble a core group of "change agents" who feel passionately about the goal.
3. **Develop a Vision**: Create strategic initiatives that inspire the coalition.
4. **Enlist a Volunteer Army**: Recruit 5–10% of the workforce to create broad buy-in.
5. **Enable Action by Removing Barriers**: Focus on removing obstacles and avoiding duplication with the existing hierarchy.
6. **Generate Short-Term Wins**: Highlight and celebrate quick wins to gain credibility.
7. **Sustain Acceleration**: Support numerous sub-initiatives to keep momentum.
8. **Institute Change**: Incorporate successful initiatives into the formal organizational hierarchy and culture.

#### The Change Management Road Map

Effective change requires implementation plans at two levels:

* **The Road Map**: A high-level master plan that identifies all improvements in sequence and transitions from project to operations.
* **Project Plans**: Detailed schedules that set deadlines and assign accountability for specific KPIs.

#### Preparing for and Executing Change

* **Identify the Need**: Change should occur because the status quo is no longer sufficient to meet organizational goals, not just because new technology exists.
* **Leadership & Incentives**: Executives must be passionate advocates. Incentives should be adjusted to a **Balanced Scorecard** (Financial, Customer, Quality, and Learning/Growth).
* **Information Sharing**: Partners must work in true partnership, which often requires diplomatic efforts to emphasize the mutual value of shared data.
* **Pilot Studies**: A successful small-scale pilot can set a benchmark and inspire confidence in other supply chain partners.

#### Continuous vs. Breakthrough Improvement

* **Continuous (Kaizen)**: Incremental, regular upgrades focused on excellence and waste reduction.
* **Breakthrough**: Large-scale, disruptive changes that often require significant structural shifts from functional to process-oriented management.

### Maintaining Technologies

#### Ongoing Maintenance and Support

Once the project team hands over the deliverables to the end users, a long-term support system must be in place.

* **Support Structures**: This includes help desks, user manuals, and technical documentation.
* **Testing Protocols**: Even after "Go-Live," technologies must be regularly tested to ensure they continue to meet quality and performance specifications.
* **Access Restrictions**: Security is maintained by strictly defining who can access the technology and which parameters they are allowed to change.

#### Maintenance Strategies

To minimize downtime and extend the life of an asset, organizations utilize three primary maintenance levels:

|Strategy|Description|Best Use|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Corrective**|Fixing equipment only after it breaks down (emergency repairs).|Low-criticality, inexpensive parts.|
|**Preventative**|Regularly scheduled maintenance (e.g., oil changes) to prevent failure.|Critical machinery with predictable wear.|
|**Predictive**|Using IoT sensors and AI to monitor real-time conditions and perform maintenance just before a failure is likely to occur.|High-value assets where downtime is extremely costly.|

#### Life Cycle Management

As technologies age, their requirements change. Maintenance managers must continuously compile data to adjust their strategy:

* **Inventory of Parts**: Revise which spare parts are kept in stock based on actual breakdown rates.
* **Depreciation**: Information on the expected remaining useful life of an asset should be shared with the accounting function to update depreciation rates.
* **Upgrade Planning**: Tracking performance degradation helps managers decide when a "patch" is no longer enough and a full technology replacement is necessary.

#### Advanced Troubleshooting

As supply chains become more automated, the causes of unexpected breakdowns are rarely obvious. Organizations must invest in **advanced training** so that maintenance teams can troubleshoot complex interactions between hardware and software rather than just physical mechanical failures.
