# Customer Relationships

## Customer Relationships and Segmentation

**Customer Relationship Management (CRM)** is a philosophy that shifts an organization from being "product-focused" to being **customer-centric**.

The road map emphasizes that CRM is not just a software solution, but a strategy to retool every business process that touches the customer—from initial acquisition to long-term retention.

### Customer Relations and Segmentation Road Map 

#### The Customer-Centric Organization

A customer-centric organization moves away from "pushing boxes" and instead focuses on providing a product/service package. Key traits include:

* **Ease of Business**: Being accessible anytime and anywhere.
* **Added Value**: Educating the customer during and after the decision-making process.
* **Innovation**: Designing services and delivery models from the customer's perspective.
* **Information Sharing**: Distributing detailed customer insights across the entire supply chain.

#### Customer Segmentation Logic

Segmentation is the practice of dividing a customer base into groups with similar requirements to increase long-term profitability.

* **Intermediate Customers**: Organizations that are not the end-users (e.g., manufacturers buying raw materials).
* **Ultimate Customers**: The final recipients of the product or service. This group is further divided between the **organizational customer** (who pays) and the **end user** (who uses).

#### Foundations of Customer-Focused Marketing

The reading highlights four fundamental concepts that drive this strategy:

* **Design Driven by Requirements**: Product features should be based on actual customer needs, not just a variety of options.
* **No Single Set of Requirements**: No one product description fits all customers; groups with similar needs must be identified.
* **Logistics Alignment**: Supply chains must be tailored to specific segments (e.g., one chain for speed, another for low cost).
* **Profitability over Volume**: Every incremental cost in marketing or logistics must be justified by a marginal increase in profit.

#### Drivers of Change

Modern customers are more expensive to win and keep due to:

* **Higher Expectations**: Customers now assume high quality and focus on price and value.
* **The Internet**: Easy price-comparison tools have turned many products into commodities.
* **Global Competition**: The marketplace has expanded, making "niche marketing" a survival necessity.

### Defining Segments

The ways to segment markets have evolved from using broad preconceptions (like age or gender) to analyzing actual buying behaviors, which provides much more accurate data for customer-focused strategies.

Segments are typically defined by three primary criteria: **Value**, **Needs**, and **Preferred Channel**.

#### Segmentation by Customer Value

Customer value is the actual or potential profit an organization makes by acquiring and retaining a customer over the lifetime of the relationship.

* **The Pareto Principle**: In many organizations, 20% of customers generate 80% of the revenue. These "Level 1" customers often have higher service needs but represent the most significant contribution to the bottom line.
* **Differential Treatment**: Leading-edge organizations treat customers differently based on this value. High-yield customers may receive better service levels, special pricing, or dedicated account management to ensure retention.

#### Segmentation by Customer Needs

This focuses on the specific services or product features that profitable segments desire.

* **Value Profiles**: Organizations analyze buying behaviors and search data to create a "value profile." While some segments prioritize price, others may prioritize reliability, convenience, or the sensory experience of a retail environment.
* **Value Propositions**: Once a profile is established, a value proposition is drafted to explain exactly how the product or service fulfills that segment's specific needs.

#### Segmentation by Preferred Channel

Technology allows customers to choose how they interact with a business. Segmentation helps identify which "channels" different groups prefer:

* **Technology Channels**: Some customers prefer self-service options (websites, automated billing, or chat services) because they are faster and provide more control.
* **High-Touch Channels**: Other segments may still require a "live" representative for complex or high-relationship sales.
* **Incentivizing Channels**: Organizations often reward customers for using less expensive channels, such as offering discounts for paperless billing or automated payments.

#### Sources of Customer Information

To define these segments accurately, organizations gather data from multiple directions:

* **Transaction Records**: Show frequency, volume, and how purchases are financed.
* **Sales and Service Reps**: Provide qualitative feedback on customer complaints and requirements.
* **Market Research & VOC**: The Voice of the Customer (VOC) is a tool used to capture actual customer descriptions of the functions and features they desire. It helps uncover unstated needs and assists in continual improvement.

### CRM Strategies by Customer Segment and Type

A **CRM Strategy** involves translating an organization’s high-level goals into specific choices regarding the **four P**s (Product, Price, Placement, and Promotion).

The roadmap for these strategies is divided into two main categories: strategies for specific **Customer Segments** and strategies for the various **Customer Relationship Types**.

#### CRM Strategies by Customer Segment

These strategies tailor the business approach based on who the customer is and how they prefer to interact:

* **Value-Based Strategies**: Organizations prioritize high-yield customers by providing "white-glove" services, such as dedicated account managers or free express shipping, ensuring the costs of improved service are balanced by increased profit.
* **Service-Minded Strategies**: Focuses on the call center as the "heart" of the business. CRM technology allows representatives to see a customer's history instantly, preventing them from having to re-educate the provider and allowing for immediate issue escalation for high-value clients.
* **Retail Strategies**: Research shows retail customers value the "bundle" of services (in-store help, easy assembly, and pre-shopping research) and quality over price. Strategies here focus on the sensory experience and post-purchase support.
* **B2B Strategies**: Business customers look for complementary core competencies. They value providers who understand their specific industry requirements and offer continuous improvement suggestions rather than just products.
* **Technology Channel Strategies**: These focus on self-service (web portals, automated tracking). The key is testing: will a tech-advanced channel drive customers away, or do they value the speed and control it provides?

#### CRM Strategies by Relationship Type

Just as products have life cycles, customers move through stages. Strategies must be customized for each:

|Relationship Type|Goal|Strategy/Tactics|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Prospective**|Acquisition|Use market research and referrals to shape the first promotional message.|
|**Vulnerable**|Retention|Use Predictive Churn Models to identify at-risk groups and offer "frequent user" rewards or discounts to stay.|
|**Win-Back**|Re-engagement|Act fast (within the first week of loss). Offer "save" programs to profitable ex-customers before they sign competitor contracts.|
|**Loyal**|Growth|Use Loyalty Programs and techniques like Cross-selling (related parts) or Up-selling (higher-tier service plans).|

#### Impact on Profitability

The reading cites a study by PricewaterhouseCoopers showing the tangible ROI of these targeted campaigns:

* **Churn Reduction (Vulnerable)**: 15% to 20% increase in profits.
* **Win-Back Programs**: 10% to 20% increase in profits.
* **Prospecting/Loyalty Growth**: 2% to 4% increase in profits.

## Customer Relationship Management

**Customer relationship management (CRM)** strategies will vary depending on what elements of CRM are selected, how the components will interact, and what technologies will be used. The organization also needs to determine how product life cycles and types of customer segments will influence the chosen strategy. CRM technologies are discussed in more detail after that, including core technologies and marketing/sales technologies. Implementation tips are also provided.

### Scope of CRM

The **Scope of CRM** defines how an organization plans to initiate, develop, and sustain customer relationships to support its overall business strategy.

It is a cyclical process that moves from functional operations to high-level strategic collaboration, all focused on the **four Ps of marketing** (Product, Price, Placement, and Promotion).

#### The Five Levels of CRM Activity

The scope of CRM is often visualized as a series of layers surrounding the core customer segment. These activities require increasing levels of skill and effort:

* **Sales Operations**: The most functional level. It includes order taking, invoicing, call centers, help desks, and returns. In a CRM context, these are optimized to collect comprehensive transaction data and improve the customer experience.
* **Analysis**: Validating and aggregating customer data into segments. This involves querying databases (or a Customer Data Warehouse) to associate specific products with the right customer groups.
* **Customer Information Dissemination**: Ensuring that analyzed data reaches the right people—such as executives, marketing teams, and web developers—to turn insights into actual business actions.
* **Relationship Building**: Developing "lifetime customers" by training staff to optimize every interaction and using data to design specific acquisition and retention initiatives.
* **Collaboration**: The most advanced level, involving joint problem-solving or soliciting product development ideas directly from customers to create mutual dependence.

#### The CRM Business Cycle

The reading emphasizes that CRM is a continuous loop rather than a linear process:

1. **Set Goals**: Define what you want to achieve for a specific target segment.
2. **Acquire**: Gain the customer or increase market share within that segment.
3. **Fulfill**: Meet the business terms and deliver the product/service.
4. **Repeat**: Use the feedback and data from fulfillment to set new goals.

#### Optimizing Sales Operations

Modern CRM transforms traditional sales tasks by focusing on two key goals:

* **The Virtual Point of Contact**: Every employee who contacts a customer (from billing to field service) should have access to the same integrated data—previous purchases, web visits, and open complaints.
* **Economic Efficiency**: While the goal is to provide what the customer values, CRM dictates doing so at the lowest cost. For example, expensive "white-glove" services might be offered for free only to the most profitable segments.

#### Key Takeaway: Relationship vs. Transaction

The scope of CRM shifts the focus from a **single transaction** to a **sustained relationship**. This is driven by the fact that lifetime customers are more profitable: they lower total marketing costs over time, are easier to satisfy due to established "learning relationships," and offer more opportunities for cross-selling and up-selling.

### Components of CRM Strategy

A **CRM Strategy** is built upon seven key components that provide an organization with a "360-degree view" of the customer. These components integrate technology, marketing, and service to turn data into a customer-centric business model.

#### The 7 Components of CRM Strategy

* **Enterprise Business Systems (EBS)**: This is the technological backbone. It often draws data from an ERP system and includes the customer database (contact info, shipping preferences), transaction maintenance (order entry and history), and financial details (accounts receivable and collections).
* **Web Systems**: These enable self-service and interaction. They allow customers to view product photos, place orders, participate in online auctions, and access online learning portals.
* **Marketing**: Considered the heart of customer management. This component ensures customers receive the right information to drive purchases and is responsible for identifying the specific values a customer expects.
* **External Data**: This supports collaborative partnerships with suppliers and resellers. It is used to design product bundles, attractive packaging, and determine the most efficient transportation choices.
* **CRM Applications**: This component is divided into three distinct elements:
  * **Operations CRM**: Portals, email, invoicing, and billing.
  * **Collaborative CRM**: Used for joint forecasting and process design.
  * **Analytical CRM**: Focused on capturing, storing, and reporting historical customer data.
* **Analytics**: This focuses on making information "actionable." It evaluates sales activities and tracks prospects to ensure business processes remain centered on the customer.
* **Service**: The final component focuses on post-sale follow-up. It includes "cyberagents" for live order assistance, electronic surveys, and web-based self-service to enrich the long-term relationship.

#### CRM Processes and Organizational Structures

To execute these components effectively, the reading highlights two supporting frameworks:

1. **Process Strategy**: Focuses on improving "time to market" by reducing non-value-added tasks in demand management and maximizing concurrent (rather than sequential) tasks. A key element is the **plan-do-check-action model** to link decisions to results.
2. **Organizational Structure**: As companies become customer-centric, they must adapt job descriptions and workflows. Teams often specialize by **campaign type** (Prospecting, Vulnerable, Win-back, or Loyal) or use **job rotation** to learn different approaches to customer management.

***Implementation Tip***: When performing job rotations, it is a best practice to have staff gain experience with **less profitable segments** first to avoid endangering high-value relationships.

### Product Life Cycle Stages in CRM

#### 1. Development Stage

The focus is on aligning the product with actual customer desires.

* **CRM Role**: Gather feedback via focus groups, social media, and buying trends to identify concepts with the highest profit potential.
* **Key Strategy**: Involve "key customers" early to create a sense of ownership and partnership, providing a source of referrals before the product even hits the market.
* **Testing**: Use CRM to select test segments and confirm if the pricing, channel, and promotions are accurate.

#### 2. Introduction Stage

At this stage, advertising costs are high as the organization works to build rapid awareness.

* **CRM Role**: Support the promotional program and ensure high satisfaction levels for early adopters.
* **Tactic**: Assign dedicated customer care teams to new owners to resolve any initial product flaws quickly and maintain brand integrity.

#### 3. Growth Stage

As the product gains momentum, the organization must distinguish between strong and weak customer segments.

* **CRM Role**: Test advertising messages for effectiveness across different groups.
* **Goal**: Sustaining customer care to manage the influx of new users and prevent high-value customers from defecting as competitors enter the space.

#### 4. Maturity Stage

This stage is characterized by intense competition and a peak in sales.

* **CRM Role**: Entice competitors' customers to switch while maintaining the existing base.
* **Tactic**: Use real-time data to identify outlets with high demand and move inventory there, while implementing promotions to sell off stock before the next cycle begins.

#### 5. Decline Stage

The focus shifts to protecting the long-term relationship even as the specific product fades.

* **CRM Role**: Provide service and replacement parts for obsolete products to maintain trust.
* **Tactic**: Use migration programs to transition customers from the declining product to the organization’s newer offerings, preserving the Lifetime Customer Value.

#### CRM Technologies Summary

To support these stages, organizations use several core technologies:

* **Customer Data Warehouse (CDW)**: A repository for analyzing customer preferences and marketplace trends.
* **Customer Interaction Centers (CICs)**: Grouped service functions (chat, email, phone) that provide a personalized experience.
* **Customer Experience Management (CEM)**: Tools that measure intangible feelings and expectations to create a superior buying experience.

### Customer Care and Marketing/Sales Technology

**Customer Service Metrics and Performance** represent the operational side of CRM, ensuring that the promises made by marketing are actually fulfilled by the supply chain.

#### Strategic vs. Operational Metrics

The text distinguishes between high-level assessments for executives and the daily details managed by supply chain teams.

* **Strategic Metrics**: These are long-term, aggregated summaries.
  * **Perfect Orders**: A total logistics metric (and a **SCOR** metric) that tracks if an order was entered, picked, delivered, and paid for correctly. Even a small increase in perfect orders (e.g., 3%) can lead to a 1% increase in total profits.
  * **Customer Satisfaction**: Benchmarking internal capabilities against best-in-class competitors to identify performance gaps.

* **Operational Metrics**: These track the daily "speed and accuracy" of the organization.
  * **Availability**: Measured by **Stockout Frequency** and **Fill Rate** (the percentage of units or dollars shipped on schedule).
  * **Lead Time Monitoring**: Tracking cycle time from order placement to delivery.
  * **Flexibility**: The ability to handle unusual requests, such as a sudden product recall or a customized marketing initiative.

#### Customer Care Technology

To maintain these metrics, organizations use **Customer Interaction Centers (CICs)** and specialized reporting tools:

* **Order-Status Reporting**: Providing customers with a portal (using **APIs**) to see if an order is received, picked, packed, or out for delivery. This reduces "status inquiry" calls to the service center.
* **Malfunction Recovery**: Having contingency plans for service breakdowns. Metrics here track the average time to recover an item from a stockout or an alternate facility.
* **Response Accuracy**: Ensuring that the first response to an inquiry is correct so the customer doesn't have to follow up a second time.

#### Marketing and Sales Technology

The reading highlights how technology acts as the "connective tissue" between the seller and the buyer.

* **Sales Force Automation (SFA)**: Automates the sales process, including lead tracking, contact management, and order entry, allowing sales teams to focus more on relationship building and less on paperwork.
* **Customer Data Warehouse (CDW)**: A central repository that stores all historical customer interactions, allowing for **Analytical CRM** (identifying trends) and **Predictive Modeling** (identifying at-risk customers).
* **Partner Relationship Management (PRM)**: A business strategy and set of tools used to increase the value of the channel network. It helps select the right sales partners and ensures everyone in the supply chain contributes to the final customer's satisfaction.

#### Measuring Satisfaction: The "Face" of the Data

A key takeaway from the text is that aggregate percentages (like "99% success") can lead to complacency. Executives often prefer to see **real numbers**—for example, seeing that "9,000 customers received late orders today" provides a much clearer motivation for improvement than a simple percentage.
