# Product Design

## Product Design Road Map

The **Product Design Road Map** establishes that product and service design must be **collaborative and holistic**, involving all functions and supply chain partners affected by the design. Design is not limited to engineering aesthetics or functionality—it spans from marketing through production and distribution.

Key ideas:

* Design converts **customer needs or innovations** into usable products, processes, or services.
* Design decisions strongly influence supply chain performance:
    * Design costs are only ~5–15% of product cost
    * About **70% of delivery cost** is locked in by design choices


* Product traits affect:

    * Sourcing options
    * Manufacturing feasibility
    * Logistics efficiency
    * Sustainability and returns

Good design balances **functionality, validity (value relative to price), efficiency, quality, serviceability, and recyclability**. Poor design can make products uncompetitive due to high cost or slow time to market.

### Traditional Design Process

The traditional (or “over‑the‑wall”) design process is **sequential and siloed**.

How it typically works:

* Marketing defines requirements and passes them to engineering
* Engineering designs the product and passes it to production and purchasing
* Production and purchasing discover cost, sourcing, or manufacturability issues
* Designs are repeatedly sent back for revision
* Logistics is involved late and may find packaging or capacity problems

This process often results in:

* Increased costs
* Longer design cycles
* Problems unknowingly built into the product
* Poor alignment with supply chain capabilities

### Collaborative Design Process

Collaborative design replaces silos with **cross‑functional and cross‑organizational teams**.

Key characteristics:

* Design teams include:

    * Engineering
    * Marketing
    * Production
    * Purchasing
    * Supply chain partners (suppliers and sometimes customers)


* Supply chain implications are considered early, while changes are inexpensive
* Partners help estimate cost, feasibility, and tradeoffs during design

The result is:

* Faster time to market
* Lower total cost
* Better alignment with customer needs
* Fewer late‑stage redesigns

### How Much Collaboration? 

Collaboration exists on a continuum:

1. **Over‑the‑wall** — no external input
2. **Informal collaboration** — advice via conversations
3. **Formal collaboration** — suppliers/customers are part of the design team

Research shows:

* Greater supplier and customer involvement generally leads to:

    * Lower cost
    * Higher quality

* The owning firm should **retain final design authority**, even when collaborating

### Implementing Design Collaboration

Collaboration does not happen automatically—it must be **intentionally implemented**.

A successful approach includes:

* Proof‑of‑concept projects to demonstrate benefits
* Formalizing terminology, training, and expectations
* Creating cross‑functional teams with assigned time and metrics
* Aligning incentives so teams benefit from supply chain cost reductions

This phased approach helps organizations adopt collaboration sustainably.

### Benefits of Design Collaboration 

Key benefits include:

* Fewer cost overruns
* Better manufacturability and sourcing feasibility
* Access to supplier expertise and innovation
* Improved customer satisfaction
* Shorter development cycles
* Higher quality at an acceptable price
  
## Broad-Based Design Methods

Broad‑based design approaches look at **overall system performance**, not isolated functions.
The three main methods are:

* Design for the supply chain
* Design for logistics
* Design for X (DFX)

### Design for the Supply Chain

Design for the supply chain means designing products with **end‑to‑end supply chain impact in mind**, from raw materials through end‑of‑life.

Goals:

* Faster inventory turnover
* Lower inventory and transit costs
* Reduced lead times
* Improved time to market

It integrates considerations related to:

* Standardization
* Simplification
* Customization
* Quality
* Sustainability

### Design for Logistics (DFL)

Design for logistics focuses on **minimizing transportation, storage, and handling costs** through product and packaging design.

Key principles:

* Efficient packaging and pallet utilization
* Unitization and containerization
* Standard box sizes
* Reduced package volume and weight

Examples:

* Stackable plastic bins
* Flat‑pack furniture (e.g., IKEA)
* Nested products

Benefits:

* Lower logistics and warehousing costs
* Better storage capacity utilization
* Faster handling and replenishment

Tradeoffs:

* Larger pallet quantities may not suit all retailers
* Weight vs. cube constraints must be balanced

### Design for X (DFX)

Design for X (or Design for Excellence) is a **holistic design philosophy**.

It means designing for whichever “X” factors are strategically important, such as:

* Manufacturability
* Assembly
* Quality
* Service
* Reverse logistics
* Sustainability
* Customization

Each organization defines which “X” priorities matter most.

## Standardization and Modularization

Standardization reduces variation by using **common designs, parts, or processes**.
Modularization builds on this by creating **standard modules** that can be assembled into different products.

Benefits:
* Lower design and manufacturing costs
* Economies of scale
* Faster product development

### Modular Design

Modular design uses standardized components to create product families.

Examples:

* Computers (RAM, drives, GPUs)
* Automobiles
* Furniture kits

Benefits:

* Reduced manufacturing and design cost
* Easier customization
* Faster assembly and logistics

Tradeoffs:

* Higher per‑unit cost than fully integral designs
* Risk of assembly errors
* Less emphasis on aesthetic integration

### Component Commonality

Component commonality uses **one part instead of many similar parts**.

Benefits:

* Lower purchasing and inventory costs
* Simplified production
* Reduced error risk

Tradeoffs:

* Design flexibility may decrease
* Potential quality compromises

### Universality

Universality designs products for **multiple markets from the start**.

Examples:

* Dual‑voltage power supplies
* One‑size‑fits‑all products

Benefits:

* Increased sales volume
* Lower design and production costs

Tradeoffs:

* Less optimized for specific markets
* Potentially shorter product life cycles

## Simplification

Simplification removes unnecessary complexity from products and services.

Benefits:

* Shorter lead times
* Higher quality
* Lower cost
* Better profitability

Key simplification approaches:

* Concurrent engineering
* Design for manufacture and assembly (DFMA)
* Design for service

### Concurrent Engineering

Concurrent engineering involves **multiple stakeholders early** in design.

Benefits:

* Shorter development cycles
* More realistic designs

Tradeoffs:

* Largely replaced by more robust methods like DFMA

### Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DFMA)

DFMA ensures products are:

* Easy to manufacture
* Easy to assemble
* Affordable
* Fast to bring to market

Goals:

* Reduce number of parts
* Minimize handling
* Avoid tight tolerances
* Simplify testing and assembly

Benefits:

* Lower cost
* Less variation and confusion
* Enables lean and modular strategies

Tradeoffs:

* May conflict with marketing or aesthetic preferences

### Design for Service (Maintainability)

Design for service focuses on **easy maintenance and repair**.

Benefits:

* Lower total cost of ownership
* Higher customer satisfaction
* Opportunites for parts and service revenue

Tradeoffs:

* May compete with aesthetics or initial cost goals

# Quality, Customization, and Sustainability

## Quality

Quality in product design is defined as conformance to requirements or fitness for use. It is a central design concern because it directly affects customer satisfaction, cost, and competitiveness.

### Key ways quality is defined:


* **Transcendent** – an ideal state of excellence
* **Product‑based** – based on measurable attributes
* **User‑based** – fitness for customer use
* **Manufacturing‑based** – conformance to specifications
* **Value‑based** – level of excellence relative to price

Quality also has two major components:

* **Quality of conformance** – absence of defects
* **Quality of design** – how well the product’s features satisfy customers

Quality must be deliberately designed into products, not inspected in later.


### Design for Quality

Design for quality focuses on using **measures and targets** to ensure the product meets customer needs in performance, aesthetics, and cost.

Key points:

* Uses both objective and subjective measures
* Organizations define what is “acceptable” vs. “exceptional”
* Quality is often an order qualifier, and in some strategies, an order winner

**Benefits:**

* Fewer defects and less waste
* Higher customer satisfaction

**Tradeoffs:**

* Higher upfront design and development costs
* Long‑term savings can be difficult to directly attribute

### Design for Six Sigma

Design for Six Sigma (DFSS) designs products and processes that meet very high quality levels (six sigma performance) while remaining cost‑effective.

Key characteristics:

* Strong focus on the **voice of the customer (VOC)**
* Prevents defects through design rather than correction
* Closely linked to continuous improvement efforts

Its primary objective is **predictable, low‑variation performance**.


### Quality Function Deployment (QFD)

QFD is a structured methodology that translates customer needs into technical design requirements.

Key features:

* Uses tools such as the **House of Quality**
* Compares customer needs against:

    * Design characteristics
    * Competitor offerings


* Uses group decision‑making to resolve tradeoffs with the customer’s priorities in mind

**Benefits:**

* Improves customer service
* Helps prioritize conflicting requirements
* Provides all benefits of design for quality

**Tradeoffs:**

* Complex to implement
* Requires strong organizational commitment and discipline

## Customization

Customization enables products or services to adapt to different **customer needs over time**. Pure engineering‑to‑order customization is expensive and slow, so organizations use structured methods that maintain efficiency.
The three main customization approaches are:

* Postponement
* Mass customization
* Glocalization

### Postponement (Delayed Differential)

Postponement delays final product differentiation until **demand is known**, shifting customization closer to the customer.

Key characteristics:

* Produces generic or modular products first
* Final configuration happens late in the process
* A classic **push‑pull supply chain strategy**

**Benefits:**

* Reduces inventory risk and bullwhip effect
* Lowers safety stock and pipeline inventory
* Improves cash flow
* Allows localized sourcing for last‑stage components

**Tradeoffs:**

* Requires redesign of products, processes, and packaging
* May increase cost if product variety is low

### Mass Customization

Mass customization combines **economies of scale** with **individual product differentiation**.

Key characteristics:

* Uses standardized modules
* Final assembly or configuration occurs close to the customer
* Often supported by postponement

Examples:

* HP printers assembled regionally
* Customized education programs, investment portfolios, and meals

**Benefits:**

* Lower inventory costs through demand aggregation
* Increased sales due to customer choice
* Efficient use of labor and skills at the point of differentiation
* Creation of local semiskilled jobs

**Tradeoffs:**

* Investment in equipment and training
* Potential tension with distributors
* Risk of quality issues if assembly is poorly designed or trained

### Glocalization

Glocalization blends global design with **local adaptation**.

Key characteristics:

* Products are designed for global markets
* Modified to meet local:

    * Regulations
    * Cultural preferences
    * Customer expectations



Examples include:

* Region‑specific fast‑food menus
* Medical devices designed for emerging markets
* Media and entertainment tailored to local audiences

### Reverse Innovation

Reverse innovation recognizes that:

* Innovations for emerging markets can succeed in developed markets
* Lower‑cost, simpler solutions can create new markets

Key insights:

* Emerging markets do not evolve the same way as developed ones
* Products designed for affordability and access can disrupt mature markets

This challenges traditional assumptions about global product design.

## Sustainable Products and Services

Sustainability‑focused design considers the **entire product life cycle**, including environmental, social, and regulatory impacts.

Three major design approaches are:

* Design for the environment
* Design for reverse logistics
* Design for remanufacture

### Design for Environment (DFE)

DFE integrates health, safety, and environmental factors early in design.

Key considerations:

* Reuse and recycling
* Reduced energy consumption
* Use of lighter materials
* Avoidance of hazardous substances

**Benefits:**

* Improved corporate reputation
* Lower legal and environmental liability
* Alignment with sustainability regulations and values

**Tradeoffs:**

* Higher manufacturing costs
* Potential compromises in convenience, durability, or safety

### Design for Reverse Logistics

Design for reverse logistics focuses on returns, repairs, recycling, and warranties.

Key design implications:

* Clear instructions to prevent user error
* Easy disassembly for repair
* Reusable or returnable packaging
* Accessible customer support

**Benefits:**

* Lower return costs
* Increased customer loyalty
* Better insight into product design flaws

**Tradeoffs:**

* Reverse flows are complex and costly
* Cannot always use the same infrastructure as forward logistics

### Design for Remanufacture

Design for remanufacture enables components to be reclaimed and reused in new products.

Key points:

* Captures value from material reuse
* Reduces dependence on raw materials
* Supports closed‑loop supply chains

Examples:

* Heavy equipment manufacturers reclaiming and reselling rebuilt components

**Benefits:**

* Lower cost to customers
* Reduced environmental impact
* Lower product development costs

**Tradeoffs:**

* Cash tied up in returned inventory
* Remanufactured goods cannot be sold as “new” in some markets
