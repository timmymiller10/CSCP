# Supplier Selection, Contracting, and Use

## Supplier Evaluation and Selection

### Purchasing Road Map

#### The 7 Functions of Purchasing

The purchasing process follows a linear flow to ensure materials and services are acquired and managed effectively:

1. **Supplier Selection**: The foundational step involving the development of criteria and value-added service requirements.
2. **Negotiation**: Reaching an agreement on terms and conditions.
3. **Order Placement**: The formal act of executing the purchase.
4. **Supplier Follow-up**: Ensuring timelines and commitments are met.
5. **Supplier Performance Measurement and Control**: Using data to track effectiveness.
6. **Value Analysis**: Evaluating the function of goods/services to reduce costs without sacrificing quality.
7. **Evaluation of New Materials and Processes**: Staying current with market innovations.

#### Sourcing Methods

Once an organization identifies its core competencies and determines what needs to be outsourced, it uses various methods to find suppliers, including:

* **Electronic Tools**: Auctions, reverse auctions, portals, and exchanges.
* **Direct Outreach**: Approved vendor lists, referrals, or prior relationships.
* **Formal Bidding**: Using Requests for Quotation (RFQs) or Invitations to Tender (ITTs).
* **Consultation**: Utilizing location-specific consulting organizations.

#### Traditional vs Supply Chain Thinking

|Feature|Traditional Thinking|Supply Chain Thinking|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Primary Goal**|Maximizing individual enterprise profit.|Long-term success for all partners.|
|**Pricing**|Adversarial; buyers want the lowest price, sellers want the highest margin.|Established cooperatively based on mutual needs.|
|**Relationship**|Short-term and transactional.|Strategic, deep, and ongoing.|
|**Focus**|Pitting suppliers against each other to drive down costs.|Integrating partners to improve overall profitability.|

### Selection Criteria

Organizations weight various factors to align with their strategic priorities. Key focus areas include:

* **Costs**:
  * **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)**: Moving beyond purchase price to include transportation, duties, and inventory carrying costs (especially regarding lead times).
  * **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: Understanding that a 1 dollar reduction in COGS increases gross margin more effectively than a 1 dollar increase in sales.

* **Alignment with Supply Chain Needs**: Ensuring offshore/onshore partners have complementary processes and technical communication capabilities (to reduce the **bullwhip effect**).
* **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) Policy**: Evaluating suppliers on human rights, safety, environmental sustainability, and community reinvestment.
* **Supplier Value-Added Services**: Looking for "world-class" service, including vendor-managed inventory, technology integration (RFID/CAD), and long-term collaboration.

### Negotiation

Negotiation is the process of agreeing on purchase conditions.

* **Types of Negotiation**:
  * **Competitive Bidding**: Used for commodities; involves RFQs, ITTs, and automated tools like reverse auctions.
  * **Direct Negotiation**: Used for complex, bottleneck, or core-competency materials where only a few suppliers exist.
  
* **Negotiation Principles**:
  * **Position-based**: Hard (adversarial) vs. Soft (concession-heavy).
  * **Principled Negotiation (Win-Win)**: A four-step interest-based style focusing on separating people from problems and using objective criteria.
  * **Triangle Talk**: A method involving knowing what you want, what they want, and proposing a mutually acceptable solution.
  * **BATNA**: Predetermining the Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement to know when to walk away.

### Contract Deployment and Management

This stage ensures the signed agreement functions as intended and delivers the expected economic benefits.

* **Deployment**: Ongoing activities to transition to new partners, including legal navigation, internal promotion of the contract, and implementing order-to-payment procedures.
* **Compliance Management**: Monitoring "off-contract" purchases (maverick spending) to recover lost savings and auditing initial invoices for accuracy.
* **Measuring Success and Avoiding Pitfalls**:
  * **Service Level Agreements (SLAs)**: Documenting specific performance terms and targets.
  * **Proactive Monitoring**: Regular measurement against expectations to ensure suppliers maintain internal controls.
  * **Ultimate Responsibility**: Ensuring the buying organization remains involved in managing the relationship even when activities are outsourced.
  * **Exit Strategy**: Maintaining formal backup plans to reduce disruption if a supplier must be replaced.

## Contracts

### Contract Definitions and Relationship Levels

The type of contract used is dictated by the depth of the supplier relationship:

* **Standard Purchase Order**: Used for market-based, transactional buying.
* **Annualized Contract**: A one-year agreement setting pricing and estimated requirements to ensure continuous supply.
* **Bilateral Contract**: An agreement where both parties make mutual promises.
* **Trading Partner Agreement**: A comprehensive, legally binding contract covering all facets of a strategic alliance or partnership.
* **CISG (Vienna Convention)**: A multilateral treaty governing international sales to remove legal barriers.

### Primary Contract Types

Contracts are categorized by how they distribute financial risk between the buyer and seller:

|**Contract Type**|Description|Risk Holder|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Firm Fixed-Price**|Price is set regardless of the seller's actual costs.|Seller (must absorb overruns)|
|**Cost-Based**|Price is tied to the cost of key inputs or economic factors.|Buyer (pays for cost increases)|
|**Cost-Plus**|Buyer pays all acceptable costs up to a specified maximum.|Buyer|
|**Incentive Contracts**|Motivates specific behaviors (quality, delivery) by sharing cost savings or providing bonuses.|Shared|

### Essential Terms and Conditions (T&Cs)

These clauses serve as the "rules of fair play" and should always be reviewed by legal counsel:

* **Pricing & Escalation**: Covers unit price, packaging, and allowed increases (e.g., raw material escalation).
* **Delivery & Order Requirements**: Specifies locations, modes of transport, lead times, and on-time delivery definitions.
* **Transfer of Ownership**: Uses Incoterms® to define when freight responsibility shifts.
* **Performance & Quality**: Outlines ISO standards, compliance documentation, and right-to-audit clauses.
* **Problem Resolution**: Protocols for corrective action and conflict resolution techniques (e.g., third-party arbitration).
* **Security & Data**: Safeguards for proprietary data and customs compliance (e.g., CTPAT certification).
* **Termination**: Specific terms for how and when a contract can be ended (e.g., 6 months' notice).

### Payment Terms and Financing

Managing **Counterparty Risk**—the risk that one party fails to honor the financial agreement—is central to contract deployment.

#### Payment Methods

* **Trade Credit / Open Account**: Unsecured, trust-based financing where payment is due after receipt.
* **Early Payment Discounts**: Incentives like "1/10 net 30" (1% discount if paid in 10 days).
* **Letters of Credit (L/C)**: A bank-guaranteed payment method common in international trade. The bank honors the seller's drafts once shipping documentation (Bill of Lading) is provided.

#### Currency Management

Global sourcing introduces **Currency Exchange Risk**, which impacts COGS and financial ratios.

* **Operating Exposure**: The volatility caused by flexible exchange rates on production and storage costs.
* **Currency Hedging**: Using financial instruments like Currency Futures (fixing an exchange rate for a future date) to provide financial stability and limit downside risk.

### Administrative vs. Physical Flow

The document concludes by distinguishing between the **Administrative Flow** (PO transmission, vessel booking, B/L receipt) and the **Physical Goods Flow** (material availability, main leg transport, and goods receipt), noting that "unexpected events" like container delays often disrupt the alignment of these two paths.

## Purchase Orders

### The Purchase Order (PO) Road Map

The process moves from an internal request to a legally binding external agreement.

* **Purchase Requisition**: An internal document requiring supervisory approval before a purchase is authorized.
* **The Purchase Order**: Once approved, the PO is transmitted to the supplier. It becomes a **legally binding contract** when the supplier acknowledges it (Contractual Offer + Contractual Acceptance).
* **Standard PO vs. Blanket PO**:
  * **Standard PO**: Used for one-time or discrete transactions; contains all specific terms and conditions.
  * **Blanket PO**: A long-term commitment for recurring items. It uses "releases" for individual deliveries, which significantly reduces administrative costs and eliminates the need to renegotiate terms for every order.

### Reconciliation and Payment (The Three-Way Match)

To ensure financial integrity, the ERP system and Finance module perform a verification process before releasing funds:

1. **Purchase Order**: What was authorized.
2. **Receiving Report/Packing Slip**: What actually arrived (checked for quantity and damage).
3. **Invoice**: What the supplier is charging.

**Note**: A "Three-Way Match" success triggers the removal of a financial **accrual** (a temporary record of debt) and authorizes final payment.

### Internal Order Tracking

Multiple departments rely on PO visibility to manage their own workflows:

* **Purchasing**: Primary trackers of status and exceptions.
* **Sales**: To notify customers of potential product delays.
* **Finance/Accounting**: To forecast cash flow and ensure accurate records.
* **Receiving/Traffic**: To schedule warehouse space, labor, and inbound carriers.

### Expediting (Supply and Transportation)

Expediting is the "rushing" of orders, which the text notes should be rare in well-managed systems as it often indicates poor forecasting.

* **Supply Expediting**: Pushing a supplier to prioritize your order or moving production into the "slushy or liquid zones" of the master schedule.
* **Transportation Expediting**: Paying premiums for faster modes (e.g., shifting from ground to air) or "rush" carrier fees.

### E-Procurement and Digital Models

The document highlights how digital marketplaces consolidate buyers and sellers:

* **Portals (SRM)**: Private, role-based websites where suppliers can see real-time requirements, available-to-promise inventory, and demand signals.
* **Trading Exchanges**:
  * **Horizontal**: Across different industries (e.g., for office supplies).
  * **Vertical**: Within a specific industry (e.g., automotive parts).
* **Online Transaction Matrix**:
  * **Auctions**: One seller, many buyers (Classic/Forward).
  * **Reverse Auctions**: One buyer, many sellers (competition to offer the lowest price).
  * **Exchanges**: Many buyers, many sellers.

### Benefits and Risks of Digital Exchanges

While exchanges offer **lower transaction costs** and **faster time to market**, they carry risks such as potential long-term loss of quality suppliers and adversarial relationships if the focus remains purely on driving down unit costs.
