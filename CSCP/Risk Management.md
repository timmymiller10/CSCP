# Risk Management and Supply Chain Risks

## Risk Management

Determining how to manage risk in the supply chain starts with a strategy and a plan. Therefore, the concepts of risk management, a risk management strategy, and a risk management plan are discussed first here. After that, we address risk management frameworks, including COSO Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) and Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC), and we present an overview of the ISO 31000 family of risk standards.

### Risk Management Road Map

**Risk Management Road Map** is essential because modern supply chains have become more exposed to risk due to the increasing pace of change, lean operations, and heavy reliance on technology.

#### Defining Risk vs. Threat

* **Risk**: An uncertainty that could *positively* or *negatively* affect business objectives.
* **Opportunity**: A risk with a positive uncertainty (e.g., an investment that could yield high returns).
* **Threat**: A risk with a negative uncertainty (e.g., hazards, lawsuits, or misfortunes).

#### The 4 Stages of the Risk Process

Supply chain managers must be able to perform these core iterative tasks:

1. **Identify the Risks**: Brainstorming all possible threats and opportunities and entering them into a risk register.
2. **Assess and Classify**: Performing qualitative (probability vs. impact) and optional quantitative (monetary value) analyses to prioritize risks.
3. **Develop a Response Plan**: Selecting the best response type (e.g., avoid, mitigate, or transfer) and assigning an accountable owner.
4. **Execute the Plan**: Implementing responses, monitoring for "triggering events," and regularly updating the risk register.

#### The "Street Vendor" Microcosm

The reading uses a street food vendor to illustrate universal supply chain solutions:

* **Redundancy**: Having a second car or a backup source for supplies.
* **Visibility & Responsiveness**: Establishing personal relationships with customers to sense shifts in loyalty or moving the stand to safer areas.
* **Adaptability**: Building a resilient business through contingency planning for weather or regulatory changes.

#### Risk Management Attitudes

* **Risk Appetite**: The overall amount and type of risk an organization is willing to pursue.
* **Risk Tolerance**: The readiness to accept a specific negative outcome to achieve an objective.
* **Risk Threshold**: The specific cutoff point where a proactive response becomes mandatory.

### Risk Management Types, Roles, and Maturity

#### Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM)

The core objective of SCRM is to reduce the negative impact of disruptions on supply chain performance. Accenture provides five principles to establish this as a discipline:

* **Integrate**: Align risk systems across the entire organization.
* **Model**: Develop an end-to-end risk model using predictive analytics and supplier continuity plans.
* **Diversify and Add Flexibility**: Use risk-adjusted strategies to react quickly to events, which is correlated with better budgeting and profitability.
* **Quantify**: Use new metrics like **Time-to-Recovery** to benchmark flexibility against competitors.
* **Perform Due Diligence**: Conduct "multi-tier" due diligence. Research shows that **40% of disruptions** originate below the Tier 1 supplier level.
* **Insure**: Protect against business disruptions. Historically, a large portion of losses from weather and pandemics were not covered by traditional asset insurance.

#### The Role of Risk Managers

Risk managers act as the architects of supply chain resilience. Their primary duties include:

* **Mapping**: Visualizing the entire chain to understand interdependencies.
* **Identifying Failure Points**: Spotting vulnerabilities before they materialize.
* **Awareness**: Creating a culture of risk awareness across all partners.
* **Governance**: Chairing regular review meetings, prioritizing funds for critical risks, and practicing contingency plans.

#### Risk Management Maturity Levels

Maturity refers to how well-developed an organization's processes are compared to the competition. High maturity is strongly correlated with lower stock price volatility.

Most organizations vary in maturity based on the **type of risk**:

|**Type of Risk**|**Maturity Level**|**Rationale**|
|:---|:---|:---|
|**Supply Risk**|Most Mature|Decades of development in SRM, spend management, and credit tools.|
|**Demand Risk**|Moderately Mature|Forecasting is established, but often fails to model risk (though **S&OP** and **CPFR** are helping).|
|**Process Risk**|Middle-Low Maturity|Tools for inventory and scheduling exist but rarely incorporate risk factors like mergers or IT failure.|
|**Environmental Risk**|Low Maturity|A new frontier; tools struggle to keep up with rapid regulatory changes and natural disasters.|

#### Risk Management Strategy: Known vs. Unknown

* Known Risks: Risks that have been identified and analyzed. These are addressed through specific response plans.
* Unknown Risks: Risks that exist but are currently unforeseen. These are typically managed by creating **reserve accounts** (funds) to handle the unexpected.

### Risk Management Frameworks

Risk management frameworks provide universal standards that can be tailored to an organization's specific strategic and tactical needs. The primary frameworks discussed are COSO ERM, GRC, and the ISO 31000 family.

#### COSO Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)

Developed by the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations, this framework focuses on integrating risk management with high-level strategy and governance. It consists of eight integrated components:

* **Internal Environment**: The "tone" of the organization, including its risk philosophy and ethics.
* **Objective Setting**: Aligning goals with the organization's mission and risk appetite.
* **Event Identification**: Recognizing potential threats and opportunities.
* **Risk Assessment & Response**: Evaluating the likelihood of events and selecting a response that aligns with the organization's appetite.
* **Control Activities, Information, & Monitoring**: Using policies, timely communication, and continuous audits to improve the system.

#### Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)

GRC is described as a "three-legged stool" where all three elements must be balanced for organizational stability:

* **Governance**: The oversight function (usually the Board of Directors) that monitors ethics, decision-making, and alignment with stakeholder needs.
* **Risk Management**: The specific methodology used to identify, analyze, and respond to threats.
* **Compliance** Conforming to laws, regulations, internal policies, and contracts.

**Key Benefit**: GRC allows organizations to take more risks to pursue opportunities because they can better quantify the costs and benefits of those risks.

#### ISO 31000 Standards

This family of standards provides principles and guidelines for managing any type of risk. Unlike some ISO standards, you cannot be "certified" to ISO 31000; it is a benchmark for best practices.

* **ISO 31000**: The core guidelines for understanding risk and developing a management policy.
* **ISO 31010**: A supporting standard focused specifically on **risk assessment techniques** (like Brainstorming, Root Cause Analysis, or Monte Carlo simulations).
* **ISO Guide 73**: A glossary that ensures everyone in the supply chain uses a common "risk vocabulary."

##### ISO 31000 Process Framework

The implementation phase follows a cyclical, iterative process:

1. **Establish Context**: Defining the scope and criteria.
2. **Assess Risks**: Identifying, analyzing, and evaluating the risk.
3. **Determine Risk Treatment**: Selecting and implementing options for addressing the risk.
4. **Monitor, Review, and Record**: Ensuring the process is documented and continuously improved through feedback.
