# Demand Analysis


## Demand Analysis Roadmap

### Purpose of Demand Analysis

Demand analysis helps organizations understand how internal plans align with **external market conditions**. It ensures supply chain strategies are flexible enough to adapt to:

* Economic shifts
* Changing customer preferences
* Competitor innovations
* Government regulation changes
* Industry disruptions

Demand can be analyzed at three levels:
1. **Long-term (Strategic)**

    * Focus: macro environment
    * Uses SWOT analysis
    * Macro environment = technological, economic, natural, and regulatory forces outside organizational control
    * Organization can adapt offerings over time but cannot influence the macro environment

2. **Medium-term**

    * Involves market research
    * Identifies external influences on demand
    * Helps shape marketing tactics

3. **Short-term (Operational)**

   * Tactical adjustments such as:

        * price changes
        * promotions
        * customer service adjustments

Demand can also be managed and prioritized operationally when supply-demand imbalances occur.

### How Demand Analysis Fits Into Supply Chain Planning

Demand must be:

1. Planned (analyzed + forecasted)
2. Communicated
3. Influenced
4. Prioritized

Linked to:

* **Operations planning & control**
* **Inventory management**
* **Logistics planning (transportation, warehousing, materials handling)**
* **Make-versus-buy decisions**
* **Total cost of ownership (TCO) evaluation**

**Critical SC Processes:**

* Developing personnel
* Building supply chain infrastructure
* Planning & scheduling supply/demand
* Identifying logistics requirements
* Developing logistics capabilities
* Executing plans

## SWOT Analysis
SWOT = Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats

### Internal
* **Strengths** (positive internal factors)
* **Weaknesses** (negative internal factors)

    
Derived from:

    - Skills, facilities, company reputation
    - External feedback (customers, suppliers)


### External
* **Opportunities**
* **Threats**

Derived from:

    - Market trends
    - Competitor offerings
    - Technology
    - Regulations
    - Economic & political conditions
    - Environmental scanning

Example threat: Suez Canal blockage (2021) → $10B in daily trade halted.

Uses:

    - Feeds into market plan
    - Helps identify demand risks and strategic direction

## Market Research
### Purpose
Understand customers, competition, and market conditions

Marketing vs Sales:
* **Marketing**: external perspective &rarr; internal organization
* **Sales**: internal perspective &rarr; external customers
1. **Finding potential markets**

Questions:

    Does anyone want this product?
    Is there unmet need?
    Is the product feasible?

2. **Analyzing markets**

Answers key questions:

    Who? Where? When? Why? What? How many?
    Used for market segmentation
    Determines best channels to reach customers

3. **Refining product design**

Research informs:

    Features
    Pricing
    SKU variations
    Customer service needs
    Return policies

**Important concept:**
* Too many SKUs → higher cost, confusion, lost profit
* Too few SKUs → lost sales
### Tools
* Surveys
* Focus groups
* Interviews
* Web feedback
* Purchased reports

### Types of Market Research
1. Market analysis

    * Market size, characteristics
    * Market segmentation
    * Competitor share
    * Impact of economy

2. Sales analysis

    * Sales patterns, consumption, market share

3. Consumer research

    * Attitudes, preferences, motivations

### Market Plan
Includes:

    Current market position
    Opportunity & issue analysis (SWOT)
    Marketing objectives & strategy
    Action plans, budgets
    Pro forma P&L
    Management controls

Used to influence:

    Supply chain network design
    Production footprint
    Inventory strategy
    Logistics strategy

Example:

* If Europe is main demand region → assemble in Netherlands to reduce duties & shipping volume.

### Demand Forecasting
Marketing tends to be **optimistic** &rarr; leads to:
    
    excess capacity
    inventory hangovers
    supply chain distrust

Cross-functional collaboration (S&OP, CPFR) helps mitigate bias.


### Competition Analysis & Benchmarking
Organizations must examine:

    Competitor offerings
    Competitor pricing
    Market share
    Unmet customer needs
    Competitor supply chain maturity

Examples:

* Haier succeeded in Nigeria by offering freezers that stay cold 100 hours during outages
* Haier entered U.S. mini-fridge market via Walmart, Best Buy, Home Depot

Benchmarking and SWOT can assess competitive positioning.

## Product Assessments & Portfolio Management
Includes review of:

* Product classifications
* Product life cycles
* SKU complexity
* Services
* Brand strategy

### Product Classifications
Two broad categories:

* **Goods** (durable, nondurable)
* **Services** (intangible, non-inventoriable)

Goods further divided into:

* Industrial goods (raw materials, capital goods, MRO)
* Consumer goods (convenience, shopping, specialty)

Strategic priorities differ by product type.

### Product Portfolio Complexity Management

More SKUs → higher:

* Purchasing costs
* Inventory carrying cost
* Service cost
* Manufacturing complexity
* Marketing confusion

Too many SKUs → profit declines
Too few SKUs → lost revenue

Companies must balance:

* Marketing desire for more SKUs
* Operations desire for fewer

### Product Life Cycle Review

1. Introduction (low volume, high growth potential)
2. Growth (rapid increase in demand)
3. Maturity (stable demand)
4. Decline (falling demand, limited market)

Life cycle affects:

* Demand planning
* Supply chain strategy
* Pricing
* Marketing

### Services Review

* Determine if services (warranty, support, training, etc.) still add value.

* When a service becomes standard across competitors = saturation → becomes an order qualifier (not an order winner)

* May require new services to maintain competitiveness.


### Brand Strategy & New Product Introduction (NPI)

Involves:

* Strong market analysis
* R&D
* Marketing planning
* Product phase-in/phase-out strategy

Forecasting for NPI relies heavily on **qualitative forecasting**.

Risks:

* Too high forecast → excess supply
* Too low forecast → lost sales

Different life cycle stages influence brand strategy:

* Growth → availability & quality
* Maturity → price & dependability
* Decline → low-cost availability

# Influencing Demand

## Plan-Do-Check-Action Model

**Plan-Do-Check-Action (PDCA) Model**:

* Four step process for quality improvement
1. Plan
    * Performance gap identified
    * Plan to effect improvement developed
2. Do
    * Plan is carried out (preferably on a small scale)
3. Check
    * Effects of the plan are monitored
4. Act
    * Results are studied
    * Determine what was learned & what can be predicted

### Demand Generation

**Demand Generation** = translating latent demand identified during market research into active demand for a product or service


## The Four Ps of MArketing and Demand Shaping

**The Four Ps**: A set of marketing tools to direct the business offering to the customer.


### 1. Product

Product includes physical goods, services, or bundled product/service packages.

* In traditional marketing, products were designed first and customer demand followed later.
* In today's customer-focused model, products are designed starting from customer needs, often for specific segments. 1
* Customization, modularity, and value-added features are common ways to differentiate products and shape demand.
* A well-designed product must:
    * Meet customer expectations
    * Deliver acceptable quality
    * Support sustainable profit margins
    * Be supported by marketing, sales, distribution, and customer service



**Demand shaping impact:**
    Product design decisions influence which products customers choose, acceptable substitutes during shortages, and how demand can be redirected within a product family. 1

### 2. Price

**Pricing** is a strategic lever tied closely to competition, perceived value, and brand identity.

* Price strongly influences demand levels but demand is not linear-higher prices can sometimes increase demand due to perceived quality or status.
* Customer-focused pricing may vary by:

    * Customer segment
    * Purchase behavior
    * Service expectations


* Examples include discounts, promotional pricing, premium pricing, or incentives for specific customers.


**Demand shaping impact:**

Price adjustments are often used to:

* Shift demand to off-peak periods
* Move excess inventory
* Manage constrained capacity
* Influence product substitution decisions

### 3. Place (Placement / Distribution)

**Placement** refers to how and where the product reaches the customer.

* Traditionally, placement focused on physical distribution channels.
* In a customer‑focused model, placement becomes a contact‑channel strategy, emphasizing convenience, service level, cost efficiency, and information flow.
* Channels may include:

    * Retail
    * Direct sales
    * E‑commerce
    * Mobile apps
    * Third‑party platforms

Effective channels must be:
* Accessible
* Reliable
* Secure
* Fast
* Convenient
* Profitable

Placement affects:

* Customer buying behavior
* Order timing
* Service expectations
* Total landed cost
* Forecast accuracy due to better visibility and feedback loops 1

### 4. Promotion

**Promotion** includes all communications designed to inform, persuade, and remind customers.

Key activities include:

* Market research and segmentation
* Targeted advertising
* Branding
* Timing of promotions
* Feedback measurement and performance tracking

Two major elements:

* **Branding**: Builds long‑term value, trust, and recognition
* **Packaging**: Serves both functional and marketing purposes (e.g., sustainability, education, visibility)

Modern promotion benefits from CRM systems and digital channels that allow:

* Highly targeted messaging
* Customized offers
* Continuous feedback loops

**Demand shaping impact:**

Promotion is often used to:

* Accelerate demand
* Smooth demand variability
* Support new product launches
* Shift customer behavior without changing the product itself

## Product Life Cycle (PLC) Stages
1. Development
2. Introduction
3. Growth
4. Maturity
5. Decline

### 1. Development Stage

* The product is designed and prepared for market.
* Activities include market research, product design, testing, and finalization.
* No sales occur, but costs accumulate due to R&D and preparation.

### 2. Introduction Stage
* The product is launched into the market.
* Sales are **low and ramp slowly** as awareness builds.
* Distribution and promotional costs are high.
* Profits are typically negative or minimal due to low volume and high startup costs.

### 3. Growth Stage

* Demand increases rapidly as customer acceptance grows.
* Sales and revenue rise significantly.
* Competitors often enter the market.
* Distribution expands and promotional spending may increase.
* Profits improve as volume increases.

### 4. Maturity Stage

* Sales growth slows, even if total sales volume is high.
* Competition intensifies; products become harder to differentiate.
* Pricing pressure and market share erosion may occur.
* This stage is typically the most profitable overall. 

### 5. Decline Stage

* Demand decreases due to market saturation, technology changes, or shifting customer preferences.
* Volumes fall and unit costs may rise.
* Profitability erodes and eventually disappears.

## Product Life Cycle Management (PLM)

**Product Life Cycle Management (PLM)** is the structured process used to **manage products from creation through end-of-life**, ensuring that products meet customer needs while supporting business, supply chain, and sustainability objectives.

PLM extends the basic product life cycle stages (introduction, growth, maturity, decline) by adding **greater managerial control, coordination, and long-term planning** across functions. 

### Purpose of PLM

PLM helps organizations:

* Successfully manage **new product introductions (NPIs)**
* Support products throughout their selling life
* Plan and execute **end-of-life and phase-out strategies**
* Maintain accurate, shared product information across the supply chain

### 1. Beginning of Life
* Research and development
* Concept creation
* Product design and engineering
* Launch and initial market introduction

This phase emphasizes: 
* Speed to market
* Cross-functional coordination
* Alignment between demand expectations and supply capabilities


### 2. Middle of Life
Covers: 
* Growth
* Maturity
* Early decline

Focus areas shift over time:

* Early middle-of-life: ensuring manufacturing and distribution capacity can meet rising demand
* Later middle-of-life: managing usage patterns, service level expectations, and cost control 

This is where products typically generate the **most profit**, making disciplined demand shaping and forecasting essential.

### 3. End of Life

Introduces activities that are implied-but not emphasized-in the basic PLC model, including:

* Phase-out planning
* Product retirement
* Warranty and service support
* Reverse logistics, recycling, or reclamation 1

From a supply-chain and demand perspective, this phase focuses on:

* Minimizing excess or obsolete inventory
* Transitioning customers to replacement products
* Protecting margins late in the cycle

### Role of PLM Systems and Tools

PLM may be supported by PLM software systems or integrated business tools that manage:

* Product versions and configurations
* Bills of material (BOMs)
* Effectivity dates for engineering changes
* Traceability by lot or serial number

These capabilities support:

* Forecast alignment during changes
* Product recalls or upgrades
* End-of-life and reverse-logistics activities

### PLM and Supply Chain Alignment

PLM closely links **demand management, marketing, engineering, sourcing, manufacturing, and logistics**. It ensures:

* New products are designed with supply constraints in mind
* Demand plans reflect product availability and capabilities
* Phase-outs occur without disrupting customers or financial results 1

PLM also increasingly includes **sustainability considerations**, such as recycling, repurposing materials, or reclaiming value from end-of-life products.
